The History and Meaning Behind Juneteenth

Juneteenth, also referred to as Emancipation Day, is a holiday celebrating the end of slavery in the United States. It is also known as America’s second Independence Day, Jubilee Day, Juneteenth Independence Day, and Black Independence Day. It is the oldest African American holiday and is celebrated on June 19th every year. This holiday commemorates the day in 1865 when Union troops seized control of Texas and liberated all enslaved people. The troops were led by Major General Gordon Granger, who informed the enslaved African Americans in Galveston, Texas of their freedom. This event occurred two and a half years after former President Abraham Lincoln signed the Emancipation Proclamation and months after the civil war ended. 

At the first celebration of Juneteenth in Texas, many African Americans heard about the Emancipation Proclamation for the first time. This day was initially honored by spiritual singing, prayer gatherings, and the donning of new clothes, which symbolizes freedom. In the span of a few years, Juneteenth was adopted as an annual holiday in other states. Juneteenth almost faded out several times in the past during World War II and the civil rights movement. However, the holiday gained traction again in the 21st century.

Before June 17, 2021, North Dakota, South Dakota, and Hawaii did not officially commemorate the holiday. Today, Juneteenth is honored in the same ways as it was in the past. Prayer and religious activities, addresses, educational activities, social gatherings and picnics, and festivities involving food and music are all common elements of Juneteenth celebrations in the United States. 

In the past, the holiday has been honored by former presidents Barack Obama, Donald Trump, Bill Clinton, George W. Bush. Many organizations, both private and governmental, have taken measures to recognize the occasion. Nike and Twitter are making great strides by compensating their employees on Juneteenth. Outside of the U.S, Juneteenth is observed to appreciate African American culture and immense contributions to society.

On June 17, 2021, President Biden signed the Juneteenth National Independence Day Act into law, making it a national holiday. When President Biden signed the act into law, he made a powerful statement: "...we must understand that Juneteenth represents not only the commemoration of the end of slavery in America more than 150 years ago, but the ongoing work to have to bring true equity and racial justice into American society, which we can do. In short, this day doesn’t just celebrate the past; it calls for action today."

Dr. Daniele Visioni

Dr. Dan Visioni is a postdoctoral researcher and climate modeler at Cornell University in New York. His latest project is called GAUSS (Geoengineering Assessment across Uncertainties, Scenarios and Strategies), a way to inform the public about the effects of stratospheric aerosol injection, a controversial geoengineering approach to the irreversible effects of climate change. With this project, “we aim to systematically evaluate the state of our knowledge on the subject,” said Dr. Visioni.  

Dr. Visioni discovered this specific area of research a bit randomly; he hadn’t heard about it until his PhD advisor proposed the topic. After further exploring, “I found out how deep the topic is: there are plenty of elements of climate modeling, risk management, societal outreach, philosophical questions, and so many chances to collaborate with many people with various expertise,” he explained. Dr. Visioni’s favorite projects, so far, have been his collaborative research projects and papers. Emailing one professor with an inquiry can lead to a chain reaction. Last year, Dr. Visioini worked on, “a [soil biogeochemistry] paper with the help of everybody!”

The job comes with freedom to explore — there’s always something new and exciting going on. It’s 40-40-20, in his words: 40% talking to people, 40% doing his own scientific work, and 20% studying and keeping himself up-to-date. One common misconception about geoengineering is that those who study the science don’t believe in limiting fossil fuels. “We know that there is only one solution to climate change: stop putting more CO2 in our atmosphere,” he explained. “What drives us is not the will to avoid that, nor a kind of hubris that we get to control the climate, but more a genuine worry that whatever we do might not be enough to avoid some of the bad things coming our way.” It's important to understand that, "we have a right to make an informed decision about it.”

Dr. Visioni’s identity as a member of the LGBTIA+ community makes him even prouder to be a researcher changing the world. “Sometimes it can be as simple as always having a pin with the rainbow flag on my lapel,” he said. “I’ve had more than one person come to me because of the pin because they felt like it was safe to talk to me about problems they might be having around their sexuality, and being open about it.”.

He continues to encourage people around him to ask questions and to never be afraid to stand up for yourself, especially when it comes to bullying in academia. “Never nod along if you don’t understand something, both in the class and when discussing career options.” He also hopes to inspire youth to continue pursuing their diverse passions. “Don’t be afraid to look around, explore, and find out what you like doing,” said Dr. Visioni. “There is no silver bullet against climate change: our hope lies in everybody working together.”

Summer Solstice 2021

This year, the Summer Solstice was observed on Sunday, June 20, signifying the beginning of Summer’s season! During the Summer Solstice, the Sun stands directly above the Tropic of Cancer and because the Sun appears to not be moving, the term Solstice is coined from the Latin words sol (Sun) and sistere (standing still). Although the Sun peaks during the Summer Solstice, Earth’s temperature does not reach its highest until approximately six weeks later because the water on our planet takes a longer time to heat and cool due to a higher specific heat.

 

Scoliosis Awareness

Scoliosis is considered an abnormal (sideways) spinal curvature, affecting approximately six to nine million people in the United States (2-3% of the U.S. population). Most cases are diagnosed in the stages of childhood or early adolescence, with symptoms ranging from uneven shoulders to changes in texture of the skin right above the spine. Spinal development, degree of curvature, and the location of where the spine curves are all matters of evaluating the types/necessary treatments for a patient. Treatment of scoliosis includes spinal bracing or even surgery that can prevent the curve from progressing and reduce the deformity.

Butterflies, Moths, and Climate Change

Recent findings suggest that of the very few species of moths and butterflies that have the ability to adapt to changing climatic conditions, most thrive best in the Nordic country of Finland. The Physics of the Canary Cry

Black Canary is best known for fighting alongside the Justice League, cleaning the crime-ridden streets in the Arrowverse, and teaming up with other DC superheroines in the DCEU's Birds of Prey. The “silver-tongued songstress” possesses the supersonic “Canary Cry” — a piercing, high-frequency scream powerful enough to propel her forward as she glides across long distances, ultrasonically incapacitating her enemies along the way. According to DC comics, her scream can cause ears to bleed from over 600 miles away, and can also kill listeners. Could this ever occur in real life?

The answer is no. But the concepts behind her power (it's known in general pop culture as the “Sonic Scream,”) are rooted in real-life physics. In fact, physics is used to create real life sonic and ultrasonic weapons, such as the Long Range Acoustic Device.

Black Canary uses her metahuman vocal cords to produce a high frequency sound that can incapacitate foes. When these high frequencies hit a human eardrum the rapid vibrations cause severe damage. Some comics claim she can produce hypersonic sounds (sounds that exceed a thousand million hertz)! Her “Canary Cry” vibrations are also very intense — there is a great amount of energy transported and the waves have high amplitudes.

One of the Black Canary’s defining traits is her unrealistic ability to soar through the air, using her cry as propulsion. Acoustic levitation is indeed a real phenomenon that uses high-intensity ultrasonic sound to counteract gravity. It has been tested on small beads and water droplets. Researchers have even experimented with dynamic control of these hovering objects! As of now, however, there is no way to blast large objects or humans into the air with just sound. Not only would this require incredibly intense sound waves, but we also do not have adequate technology to transform sound into thrust.

In reality, the closest humans we have to Black Canary are the current Guinness World Record holders for “highest notes sung by humans.” Wang Xiaolong can sing at 5243 Hz, far below the ultrasonic hearing threshold of 20,000 Hz. While Black Canary’s metahuman abilities are improbable in the modern world, the physics concepts integrated into her story are relevant as scientists make new discoveries in sound, waves, sonomechanics, and sonochemistry. It also makes us wonder: how would we protect ourselves from large scale ultrasonic attacks? While Black Canary’s (literally) mind blowing superpowers aren’t real, it’s interesting to see superhero stories so grounded in physics. Keep this in mind when you go and watch the next DC movie or Marvel movie coming out in theaters!

For our second installment of inveSTEMgations, we’ve decided to tackle one of the most famous and highly debated conspiracy theories. Was the moon landing real? Now there is a TON of different theories surrounding this historical event. For this chapter of InveSTEMgations, we are only going to focus on the scientific possibilities (or impossibilities) of the 1969 moon landing. 

On July 20, 1969, about half a billion people watched Neil Armstrong say “That’s one small step for a man, one giant leap for mankind”. The moon landing was an incredible feat for science, technology, and history. Just a few years after that fateful day, people began questioning the validity of this event. One of the first things people noticed that lent to the fake moon landing conspiracy is the flag. When Buzz Aldrin and Neil Armstrong planted the American flag on the moon’s surface, it seemed to be waving in the wind. Unfortunately, there is no wind on the moon due to its’ lack of atmosphere. Therefore, how could the flag possibly wave on the moon’s surface? According to Rick Feinburg, the press officer for the American Astronomical Society, the flag ‘waving’ in the wind has a simple engineering explanation. A horizontal rod was inserted into the flag so that it would look more aesthetically pleasing in pictures. Unfortunately, when the flag was planted into the moon’s surface, the astronauts had some trouble fully extending the rod, which accounts for the ripples and waviness appearing on the flag. 

Another issue people found with the moon landing was the different shadows that appeared in the images. How is it physically possible for certain objects to be seen even though they were in shadows? Since the sun is really the only source of illumination in space, there should be no way to view those objects if they were in shadow. Theorists believe that there must be other sources of light in order to see these objects, and therefore, the moon landing must have been conducted on a Hollywood set with different lights. However, the sun is not the only source of light out in space, technically. The moon’s surface also reflects light from the sun. So even though some objects are in shadow, they can still be seen due to the reflection from the moon. 

Overall, there are a ton of other theories that draw suspicion to the validity of the moon landing. Coming from a scientific standpoint though, it seems like everything was real.

A Fine Line Between Appreciation and Appropriation

Cultural appropriation is the [sub]concious act of adopting another person’s culture. As a negative form of cultural learning, many celebrities, tiktokers, and even historical “icons” have been labeled as appropriators. Whether it be the adoption of a culture’s fashion or design -- sexualizing, selling, and taking over a culture -- is a close-minded practice exhibited by people around the world. 

Using cultural items (jewelry, clothing) with any form of cultural significance and meaning, as a fashion statement, is cultural appropriation. The Kimono Fashion-Trend is a perfect example of a cultural symbol adapted to a fashion-statement and corporate greed. As profits on this “trend” increased with buyers worldwide, people started realizing that wearing and buying the kimono as a profitable item, and not for its cultural symbolism, is a form of cultural appropriation. It’s the subtle tokens in life that strictly can be identified as cultural appropriation. But, what if you taught yourself about Japanese Culture, heard the stories of Japenese-Americans, or took part in cultural exchange? This is where we can start drawing a line. 

Cultural Appreciation is defined as the literal acceptance and appreciation of another person’s culture. Let’s look at this from the perspective of the Kimono Fashion Trend. If you learned about the cultural and historical significance of the Japanese Kimono, whether it be its vitalism during World War II or educating yourself on the importance of this item with Japanese designers, you have drawn the fine line between appreciation and appropriation. However, profiting off of “modernized” Kimonos or using this item as a fashion-statement, does to exemplify cultural appreciation. 

Don’t forget, Cultural Appropriation happens in the STEM-field too! From research in rural areas to aid in disadvantaged communities, a majority of STEM advancements rely on acceptance and cooperation with new cultures. Becoming an open-minded individual and educating yourself on the vast history of those cultures is vital to the future of STEM. It is essential to ensure the people and yourself that you are not taking advantage of one’s culture, overlooking their insight, or establishing cultural dominance and superiority. A common example is the building of a highly-recommended STEM facility on top of sacred grounds in one’s culture. We spend approximately one-third of our entire lives asleep and though there has been countless research on sleep, the fundamental reason why we sleep is still unknown. 

One theory is the inactivity theory which suggests that it may have been advantageous to be inactive at night in order to avoid predators. This implies that what we now define as sleep stems from a behavioral adaptation that aided in our evolution.

Another theory is the energy conservation theory which suggests that sleep conserves energy at times when it is least efficient to search for food. Many scientists consider this theory as an extension of the inactivity theory.

Other scientists argue for the Restorative theory, which states that sleep ‘restores’ what our body loses when it’s awake. This idea has gained recent support through empirical evidence found in  various research studies that state “muscle growth, tissue repair, protein synthesis, and growth hormone release occur mostly, or in some cases only, during sleep”

The last theory is the Brain Plasticity Theory which suggests that “sleep is correlated to changes in the structure and organization of the brain.”

Although there has been no definitive conclusion for why we sleep, it is apparent that sleep is vital for adequate cognitive function and for our overall well being.

We all know that the pandemic has taken a toll on each and every one of us. The novel coronavirus has ravaged the country and the world and most of us are ready for a new beginning. Or at very least, a vaccine. But before the widespread use of vaccines and standard preventative measures, the viral lethality was even higher. So how did the world begin to improve healthcare measures and vaccine creation? For that, we need to begin our search in ancient China.

Many credit Dr. Edward Jenner as the “Father of Immunology” from his development of the smallpox vaccine in 1796, though many had attempted to utilize variolation, or the method of inoculation used to graft immunity to smallpox, centuries before. Variolation stems from the word variola, which commonly referred to as the smallpox virus. Jenner developed his vaccine after he discovered that milkmaids did not develop smallpox because of their exposure to a much less severe virus called cowpox. Cowpox was much less lethal than smallpox and only resulted in minor maladies. Because cowpox was similar in nature to smallpox, those who contracted the former were practically immune to the latter. After widespread inoculation using the cowpox disease in non-immune patients, smallpox drastically decreased in spread in 19th century Europe. 

However, it’s important that we do not perpetuate the erasure that is so often written in the history books as it relates to women in the sciences. For example, one woman(likely a nun) in Sichuan, China discovered that those who contracted the disease once never contracted it again.(It must be noted that theories on whether this physician was a male or female have been debated for centuries-for the purpose of preventing erasure of women’s achievements to the sciences, this individual will be addressed as such.) She then hypothesized that if non-immune patients were given a non-lethal dose of the smallpox virus, then they would not die from it. Because smallpox(variola major) had a lethality at the time of around 30% according to the World Health Organization, this hypothesis had the ability to save many lives. She supposedly gave her patients smallpox scabs from former carriers and placed it in the noses of those who wished to be inoculated. When she tested this theory, only around 3% of her patients died, which was much better than the nearly third of the population who would have lost their lives otherwise. Because this woman’s name and history isn’t documented, it’s unknown when exactly this occurred. Although it was originally theorized that this woman lived some time in the 1000s, the only proof we have is that by the Ming Dynasty, variolation techniques such as this became widely used to prevent the smallpox vaccine.

Understanding the nuances of medical history is an essential component of studying scientific concepts such as virology, immunology, and biology. Although practices by European physicians have aided the world in the fight against pandemics and epidemics, we must also acknowledge those who have contributed wholeheartedly to the sciences from more than a eurocentric lens. We acknowledge and thank the men and women in both the past and present that have fought day and night to save the lives of the common people. Each of them have made history in their own right and deserve to be seen as such.

How Affirmative Action affects Your College Application

Affirmative Action is a policy that aims to increase the representation of underserved and marginalized communities in higher education systems across America. The term was first introduced by John F. Kennedy in 1961, who ordered government officials to, “‘take affirmative action’ to realize the national goal of ‘nondiscrimination.’” Since then, it has sparked fierce debate during every college application season. 

Implemented in 1969, Affirmative Action had become a highly controversial topic, especially when it came to the college application process. All colleges and universities look at your applications, both holistically and academically. They will primarily look at your school statistics, community involvement, and other activities that define you. Most importantly, colleges desire students who reflect their campus and their ideals. Schools that implement affirmative action policies promote the further acceptance and education of underserved students, [sometimes] despite academic and holistic strength. 

Whether or not this is an equal system, it is crucial to understand that many colleges evaluate students based on race and historical background. Because Affirmative Action is a preferential system that gives underrepresented students a “leg up” in the admissions process, it is deemed disadvantageous for historically majority groups. Critics of Affirmative Action and believers of “reverse racism,” claim that the system snags the spots of what would have been “more qualified” students, due to the leniencies on academic success (SAT, grades, etc.). However, this does not permit anyone to discriminate against another when it comes to college admissions. If the admission committee believes you should be there, you should be there. 

The policy is vital, particularly in the STEM community. Students of color are not equally represented on college campuses due to disproportionate education systems in underfunded communities. The admissions policy permits the promotion of diversity on school campuses while helping schools reach equality in the admissions process. Ensuring that every student has an equal opportunity to STEM education, affirmative Action advances people of color to further their education. Of course, academic and holistic capability are incredibly indispensable to the college process; however, affirmative action policies do not guarantee direct admission. The system was established to help the historically discriminated, not to incriminate individual success. 

In light of conversation on the merits and shortfalls of Affirmative Action, many have proposed alternative ideas for a more beneficial and “fairer” approach. Most suggest a system based on socioeconomic status, which theoretically, would continue to boost college diversity as minority students in America face the highest rates of poverty and economic disadvantage. With the next college application season approaching, it is essential to educate each other on the importance of Affirmative Action, its global implications, and, most importantly, your college application.

The Importance of Inclusive Sex Education 

As the back-to-school season draws near, it becomes about time for teachers to revisit their lectures and revitalize their lesson plans. However, one subject in particular seems to have differing curriculums from state to state. Sex education has become a controversial topic, that either appeals to equitable education or defies the importance of key aspects of the learning environment. As a topic that has been debated for years, new extensions of the curriculum, like inclusive sex education, has proposed a larger dilemma. In essence, inclusive sex education means incorporating previously overlooked LGBTQ-based lesson plans into the regular curriculum, destigmatizing topics like gender fluidity, gender identity, sexual anatomy, and menstruation for all youth. 

The benefits to a comprehensive, inclusive sex education system are wide and plenty. Since current sex education is usually centered around heterosexuality, many LGTBQ students are isolated and left in the dark about the non-heterosexual aspects of sex and sexual health, causing these students to be more likely to have sex when younger and be at higher risk for sexually transmitted infections (STIs). Inclusive sex ed could give all students the opportunity to learn about consent and how to safely have sex, all while increasing representation of LGBTQ identities. Heterosexual and cisgender youth are also more likely to accept LGBTQ individuals, fostering a safer and more welcoming school environment. 

Six states in the US -- Alabama, Louisiana, Mississippi, Oklahoma, South Carolina, and Texas -- still have laws prohibiting schools from teaching LGBTQ topics positively in sex education classes which could create an unsafe school atmosphere. As a result, LGBTQ students from these states are more likely to have less support in their school community.

Although an inclusive sex education system would offer more support to LGBTQ individuals, opposing arguments claim that sex education, in general, is inappropriate for students. Individuals also disagree with the institutionalization of sex education in schools, simply because, students should be learning the importance of abstinence. On top of that, introducing legislation or educational bills that promote the LGBTQIA+ community through inclusive sex education, is claimed to spark “negative” behaviors in students around the nation. 

School districts throughout numerous states are still questioning the importance of Sex Education, and the vitalism of inclusivity of the LGBTQIA+ community in the school curriculum. With opposing arguments claiming the deterioration in student behaviors, and the supporting sides promoting the embracement of gender fluidity, more research has yet to be explored in terms of the impacts on equitable education, and the advancement of an inclusive mindset in generational youth leaders. Whether or not you agree with inclusive sex education, it can play a vital role in open-mindedness and acceptance in future endeavors.

What makes us Addicted to Tik Tok?

We’ve all been there. It’s 1 pm on a random afternoon. You’re trying to be productive and think “let me take a quick TikTok break”. Before you know it, hours have passed, your work is incomplete and you’ve seemingly wasted an entire afternoon. But why? What is it about this social media app that makes it so captivating and addictive. Is it the quick-witted fifteen to sixty-second videos? Is it the subconscious need to keep up with all the current trends in fear of missing out? Is it that they’ve perfectly crafted an algorithm that is designed to meet your specific niche of interests? Maybe so. However, there may also be a deeper and slightly more insidious reason why TikTok has seemingly gotten an entire generation hooked.

In an interview with Dr. Julie Albright, a Sociology professor from the University of Southern California, she says, “When you’re scrolling ... sometimes you see a photo or something that’s delightful and it catches your attention. And you get that little dopamine hit in the brain ... in the pleasure center of the brain. So you want to keep scrolling.” Your brain is programmed to seek pleasure and the slight rushes of dopamine released when watching a funny TikTok, makes your brain want more.

Furthermore, the TikTok algorithm works in a way that is oddly similar to casino slot machines: “In psychological terms [it’s] called random reinforcement. It means sometimes you win, sometimes you lose. And that’s how these platforms are designed”. There is no perfect pattern to the videos you’ll find funny on your ‘for you page’ and which ones you won't, making TikTok that much more addictive. 

This begs further questions on how technological use is changing the biochemistry of the brain and the lasting effects social media will have on preadolescent and adolescent brains. However, though there’s been a concern about the overall effects of social media, the answer on whether it is definitively good or bad is much more nuanced. From increased access to information to diversifying one’s perspective, social media has an array of benefits but maintaining a healthy balance is critical.

COVID-19 Vaccines Myths Debunked

The COVID-19 pandemic has drastically altered all of our lives yet with both Pfizer and BioNTech releasing a vaccine with an over 90% effective rate, many people are hoping that this pandemic can finally come to an end. However, with any new technology and with the increase in anti-vaxxers, these new vaccines have been met with a lot of skepticism. In this article, we will be explaining common misconceptions regarding the COVID-19 vaccines.

“The vaccine was too rushed”

Because the pandemic is a global health crisis, scientists were able to receive the adequate funding and resources necessary to speedline a process that generally takes years. Without the barriers most clinical trials face and an influx of funding, the brightest scientists in the world were able to create a safe vaccine.

“There hasn’t been enough testing”

Phase 3 clinical trials, which is required before heading to market, require the vaccine to be tested on 300-3000 people. The Pfizer and BioNTech Phase 3 trial had over 42,000 participants.

“We don’t know what’s in it; it will give you COVID-19”

Most vaccines are created using a small amount of the weakened virus it is trying to protect against. However, the COVID-19 vaccines are made using mRNA which is non-infectious, takes less time to manufacture, and can target multiple diseases. Though this technology is relatively new, it has been studied for the past decade and much of the technology used to create the COVID-19 vaccine was already created during the SARS epidemic; we did not have to start from scratch.

With the first person to have received the COVID-19 vaccine outside of clinical trials and many more vaccines being delivered worldwide, we might be seeing the beginning to the end of this pandemic. However, since many places are seeing a second wave the weeks following Thanksgiving, it is still important to continue wearing a mask, social distancing, and staying safe.

1816: The Northern Hemisphere’s “Year Without a Summer”

As we experience a year of intense political, social, health, and climate crises, we must recognize that for centuries such occurrences have existed. One such crisis in mind was the Year Without a Summer, dubbed as such due to the incredibly low temperatures experienced by the Northern Hemisphere in 1816. Let’s analyze what and how it happened.

If you’re looking in love with the wintertime, you may view this year as one for the books. For the rest of us, it may be the opposite. 1816 has been dubbed the Year Without a Summer, and for good reason. From April to August 1816, Mount Tambora in Indonesia erupted, completely layering hundreds of miles of the Indian Ocean and the atmosphere in ash. Although taking about a few months to make any impact on the Western Hemisphere, Mount Tambora’s eruptions influenced the global climate for ages.

By 1816, the ash had spread throughout the atmosphere so thoroughly that it had blocked the Sun throughout the North Hemisphere. An intense winter took place, with several contemporary accounts claiming that frost and intense snow killed crops throughout the year, even extending into August. Due to the millions of tons of sulfur dioxide and ash release from Mount Tambora, the world was in a climate crisis like no other. This resulted in a sharp decrease in temperatures of as much as 3 degrees Celsius. Without proper spring and summer conditions, blights, famines, and diseases quickly followed throughout the world. 

Within the next few years, the ash particles began to fall out of the atmosphere, eventually allowing the Sun to once again return the world to normal climate conditions. But one must recognize the achievements that came out of this climate crisis, such as Mary Shelley’s inspiration for writing Frankenstein or Karl Drais’s creation of the bicycle. Shelley wrote her novel while at Lake Geneva in the midst of the Year Without a Summer, which encouraged her to write about the gloomy setting of the novel. Karl Drais likely created the bicycle to encourage Germans to travel Europe without the need of a horse. This is because horses in Central Europe became incredibly expensive after oats and grains needed to feed them became scarce due to the lack of normal summer conditions. This indirect occurrence inspired an invention used today, along with literature, art, and other subjects in the humanities.

On December 14th, the electoral college voted to confirm Joe Biden’s win against Donald Trump, with a total of 306 electoral votes to 232. However, Biden has been selecting his Cabinet choices since November 24th, and the diversity of the group of advisors and heads of federal departments is groundbreaking, with women, people of color and members of the LGBTQ+ community in positions never held before. Biden is delivering on his promise to have a Cabinet that “looks like America'' but many Democrats debate whether or not the Cabinet has progressive enough philosophy -- it may look the part, but the lack of forceful progressives in an attempt for normalcy and stability have left some dissatisfied. 

Biden’s economic team is dominated by women, with the widely supported Janet Yellen serving as the first female Secretary of Treasury; she also was the first woman to run the Federal Reserve. Cecilia Rouse is serving as the first African American to run the Council of Economic Affairs and served under both Obama and Clinton. Finally, Katherine Tai was chosen to be the U.S. Trade Representative and is the first woman of color in the position. For the National Security team, Biden chose retired four-star General Lloyd Austin to serve as the first African American Secretary of Defense. Alejandro Mayorkas is the first immigrant and Latino Secretary of Homeland Security, and Avril Haines is the first woman to serve as the Director of National Intelligence. Other groundbreaking Cabinet nominations include the appointment of Xavier Becerra as the first Latino Secretary of Health and Human Services, as well as Deb Haaland, the first Native American Secretary of Interior, who was also one of the first Native American congresswomen. Finally, Biden selected Pete Buttigieg as the Secretary of Transportation, the first openly LGBTQ+ permanent cabinet member, and the only one of Biden’s former rivals to be nominated to the Cabinet thus far. The lack of former opponents in his Cabinet picks was quite noticeable, especially with Senators Elizabeth Warren and Bernie Sanders passed over for more viable candidates.

This philosophy held by many Republicans will block Biden’s attempts for a more progressive Cabinet, but having representation across marginalized communities in some of the highest positions of power is a step in the right direction towards equitable governing.

 
 

Niba Nirmal

For this week’s STEM Spotlight, STEMlights interviewed Niba Nirmal, creator of Notes by Niba and a multimedia science communicator based in San Francisco, California. Niba recently graduated from Duke University, studying all things green as a plant geneticist. Since June 2020, she has been working as a freelance science communicator — explaining science concepts through YouTube videos, articles, social media posts, and more. 

Niba discovered the world of science communication through one of her classes in graduate school. As someone who was already constantly writing (with a collection of 50+ journals), she immediately gravitated towards the video production and social media elements of SciComm. “You want to reach people who haven’t already been reached,” she said, citing America’s high illiteracy rate in adults. Social media — which she explored as a model in college — puts science on a medium that is much easier to reach.

Transitioning into an entirely new field without much formal media training proved to be difficult, however. For Niba, it required much self-starting. “I treated the process as a science experiment,” she told STEMlights, using her YouTube channel to experiment with the video-editing process. Although her very first video took six weeks to edit, she persisted. Niba was also selected as a 2020 Jackson Wild Fellow, attending the Jackson Wild Summit, where she connected with other science communicators and took classes with mentors from National Geographic, BBC, and other global media platforms. “If you know what you wanna do, that gives you such a huge advantage.”

Niba’s days largely consist of social media work and networking. “A lot of it has been word of mouth,” Niba said. When you’re your own boss, there’s freedom and creative license. Time and effort are central to the process. “Ultimately, it’s about communicating science to another person. I’ve met science communicators that haven’t finished high school but still do excellent work, impact communities, and even get featured by the New York Times!”

What makes Niba especially unique is her interest in merging science with beauty/ fashion, although she understands that it may invite many problems, especially as a woman of color. Like many other WOC in the field, she has experienced negative comments about how she presents herself — even when it’s just a button-up and glasses (see photo above). “I use it as a perfect example of the kind of BS we face,” she explained. “It’s all the more reason to do it because then I get people who say, ‘oh that’s cool,’ and that means so much to me.”

Her favorite fact to share? Today’s banana flavoring for packaged sweets is based off of an extinct version of the fruit, which is why it sometimes has an “off” taste. 

Niba is the embodiment of dedication, drive, and commitment to inclusivity/mentorship. It was a pleasure to chat with her and learn more about SciComm as a field. To learn more about Niba, be sure to visit notesbyniba.org, her LinkedIn, YouTube, and Instagram!

 
 
 

How Fast Does a Snowflake Fall From the Sky?

The majority of snowflakes (approximately 95%) fall at speeds ranging from 1 to 6 feet per second, even in the case of high winds. The main factors that affect the speed of a falling snowflake are its surface area and weight. The more compact a snowflake is, with a smaller surface area, the faster it falls. In addition, a snowflake can fall faster when the surrounding temperature is above freezing, resulting in wet (heavy) snowflakes.

More Facts About Snowflakes:

The reason why snowflakes have hexagonal shapes can be attributed to the way molecules in ice crystals join together, in a hexagonal fashion. This type of arrangement provides the greatest amount of efficiency for water molecules to structurally form themselves. In addition, snowflakes can be classified into different types. There are dendrite snowflakes, thin plate snowflakes, and sector plate snowflakes.

What is Reindeer Vision?

The vision capabilities of Arctic Deer is quite unusual. Reindeers have the ability to see ultraviolet light, which is invisible to most humans and animals. The reflective tissue in their eye, which turns blue during the Winter months, adds on to these unique vision capabilities. With the help of these “special” eyes, Arctic Reindeer can easily spot plant food sources or predators hiding in the snow.

Welcome back to “Sci-Fi or Fact,” where we explore the intersection between pop culture and STEM! In this edition, we’ll delve into the science of the movie, Home Alone, and its many seemingly death-defying stunts. 

Just to recap, Home Alone is a renowned Christmas classic about a boy, Kevin, left alone in his house only to end up defending his home from a pair of burglars through a series of booby traps. One such significant booby trap was the heating of a doorknob handle with an electric charcoal lighter, to the point where the doorknob on the other side glew bright red. In this scene, the burglar, not realizing the high temperature of the doorknob, burns his hand. However, as shown in a video by Vsauce3, it seems very unlikely that the doorknob on the other side would heat up fast enough to warrant a severe burn. Another memorable booby trap was when Kevin smacked both burglars with a paint can to the face, which compared to the previous booby trap, could actually work. With a 10 feet long rope and a full paint can on hand, that elaborate trap could’ve caused the burglars to be hit with roughly 2 kilo-newtons.

Now, how did the burglars manage to stay conscious and mobile after experiencing these lethal-looking obstacles? Well, the short answer is that they probably couldn’t. According to Dr. Ryan St. Clair of the Weill Cornell Medical College, grabbing a scorching hot doorknob would’ve been enough to cause third-degree burns, increasing the burglar’s risk for infection. Through contracture, his hand would have also essentially been rendered useless since the resulting scar tissue could potentially decrease the flexibility/movement of that limb. Similarly, receiving a 10-pound paint can to the face head-on would’ve definitely fractured multiple facial bones, caused a mind-numbing concussion and probably knocked out some teeth. 

Good thing these stunts only occurred on the Hollywood screen, or else, Kevin might’ve been accused of murder! Regardless, it’s interesting to see how much movie magic could change one’s perception of science and its impact in a few scenes. We hope everyone has a safe and healthy holiday season, and if you find yourself home alone, defending your house from a pair of burglars, make sure to be a little more careful about which elaborate traps to set up.
