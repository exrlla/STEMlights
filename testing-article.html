Trump vs. Biden: A Platform Comparison

The clock is ticking, as the presidential, congressional, and local elections are just over one week away. And as of October 24th, over 56 million Americans have already voted, whether it be through mail-in ballots or in-person. We are providing a comparison of Biden’s and Trump’s plans, policies, and stances on issues important to voters, split up between public health, civil liberties, and economics. All statements or quotes come directly from the Biden campaign’s website, Trump’s Promises Kept website, interviews, or the first and second presidential debates. 

Key issues under the topic of public health include COVID-19 and health care. Biden’s platform regarding coronavirus consists of an increase in the supply of front-line workers’ personal protective equipment (PPE), test and trace methods, an increase in the development of the vaccine, and encouraging all to wear masks. Trump supports reopening the country to boost the economy and has stated the vaccine is in the works and will be available within weeks to months. After contracting COVID-19 himself, Trump said “don’t be afraid” of the virus, and during the first presidential debate, he criticized Biden for wearing “the biggest mask I’ve ever seen". Regarding health care, Biden supports the Affordable Care Act with some additions such as increasing transparency, decreasing price, and providing more options. Trump has attempted to repeal the Affordable Care Act, citing it as an “economic burden” and instituted a rule allowing workers to refuse to perform abortions, sterilizations, and assisted suicides on religious grounds. He has not laid out a comprehensive health care plan but states he has expanded several healthcare options. 

Racial injustice, including Black Lives Matter and immigration, is a significant issue that falls under civil liberties. Biden supports the Black Lives Matter movement and plans to “reform criminal justice” and “advance the economic mobility” of Black Americans to combat educational and health disparities. Biden stated that he is “totally opposed to defunding the police” but wants to address “systemic police misconduct”. Trump has called the Black Lives Matter movement “a symbol of hate” and supports increased police funding and presence in communities. During the last presidential debate, Trump stated that he was “the least racist person in this room," citing accomplishments in prison reform and opportunity zones. Regarding immigration, Biden plans to protect Dreamers and DACA, repeal unethical travel bans, and keep ICE accountable. Trump continues to promote the border wall, and his platform relies on combating “illegal alien crimes," ending catch and release, chain migration, and the visa lottery, and enforcing strict immigration laws. 

Finally, the subject of economics includes jobs, the wealth gap, and climate change. The Biden plan for rebuilding job opportunities includes providing relief to "working families, small businesses, and communities," bringing manufacturing back to America, and not raising taxes for those who make under $400,000 a year. During the first presidential debate, Biden stated, "I do not support the Green New Deal… I support the Biden plan." This plan focuses on promoting green jobs with an emphasis on people of color and middle to low-income households, addressing the wealth gap, as well as rejoining the Paris Climate Accords and focusing on sustainable transportation, infrastructure, energy, housing, and agriculture. Trump plans to stay out of the Paris Climate Accords and promotes the expansion of oil and gas companies as well as bringing other manufacturers to America to boost the economy. Trump’s position on the existence of climate change is unclear, but he is against clean energy sources like solar and wind due to their lack of viability and their impact on oil and gas companies. Regarding the economy, the Trump platform promotes tax cuts for corporations and businesses, “low regulation," and a plan to “cut middle-income taxes”. 

Regardless of political views, all eligible Americans must vote and educate each other on the issues that matter the most. Go to vote.org for your local polling place, drop box, and ballot information. 

 
 
 
 
 
 

Elaine Kharbanda

A first-year college student at Columbia University, 19-year-old Elaine Kharbanda founded LetsGapp, “the digital hub for everything gap year.” LetsGapp’s mission is to connect gap year students across the globe—providing resources and building a supportive community of these so-called gappers.

For her own gap year, Kharbanda ventured to Italy, South America, India, and her dream destination, Antarctica. Then, after a six-continent journey, she spent five months stranded in New Zealand due to the coronavirus pandemic. 

"A gap year can be a very individual process, and while this experience enables gappers to become more independent and learn more about themselves, it also means that it can get lonely. In fact, the FOMO of seeing your friends at college is a pretty common reason that students decide against taking a gap year,” Kharbanda told STEMlights. With this in mind, she created LetsGapp Connect, their program that fosters dialogue by grouping gappers with shared interests. To get matched, all you have to do is sign up.

It joys her to hear positive feedback; people have been making new connections and staying in touch with their matches. “l’ve been able to take a step back and really think about what is important to me in my life. I’ve also met so many amazing people throughout my gap year and in the gap year community,” she explained.

At Columbia, Kharbanda plans to major in Chinese and Economics. When not bungee jumping or working on LetsGapp, she is involved in Columbia’s Formula Racing Team and Bacchanal, a student-run organization and publication focused on music.

Her advice for future gappers? Make a list of what you want to accomplish. “Seek discomfort,” she said, which doesn’t always mean taking things to the extreme. Explore the food, activities, and skills you’ve always wanted to. “Your gap year allows you to spend your time on your own terms, so think about how you can make every second count.”

Learn more about LetsGapp: letsgapp.com, @letsgapp. Follow Elaine on Instagram @elainekharbanda

In this edition of Sci-fi or Fact, we’ll be exploring the relationship between music and politics. Historically, music has consistently been an important factor in influencing political movements, but why? What’s the science behind this prominent figure of political change? Let’s dive right in!

Even the classical Greeks from long ago recognized the monumental impact of music. As Plato stated, “When modes of music change, the fundamental laws of the state always change with them.” They regarded it as an art form controlled by the Muses, helping to incite passions and inducing states of mind that could affect emotion and health. In recent years, just in the United States, Billie Holiday’s “Strange Fruit” decried the lynchings in the American South, and Bob Dylan’s “Blowing in the Wind” rallied individuals against the Vietnam War, becoming one of the most popular anti-war songs of all time. In Australia and New Zealand, popular songs written by indigenous songwriters jump-started an indigenous land reclamation movement. In South Africa, Mbatanga music aided in ending apartheid, spreading themes of peace and unity. In Chile, the Nueva Cancion movement was sparked by Victor Jara’s songs, inspiring South Americans to rebel against military dictatorships. Clearly, all around the world, music has been and is still a core aspect of social revolution, but why?

From a human perspective, music evokes indescribable emotion within people. It gets people thinking and then eventually doing, unifying individuals towards achieving a common goal. Joan Baez, a folk singer and political activist, once said in an interview, “I think that music is probably the only medium that really does cross all boundaries, and all languages, and all countries.” These melodies and rhythms form a sense of collective identity and can fuel one’s want for change.

From a scientific perspective, however, the reasoning behind music’s effectiveness is still unclear. Many believe that music originated as a tool to communicate within a group, while others believe that music, through dancing to different beats, was related to courtship. In 2011, Robert Zatorre, a neuroscientist at McGill University, found that when listening to music, dopamine was released in the brain, affecting arousal, motivation, and reward. Frances Rauscher and Gordon Shaw, respectively of UW-Oshkosh and University of California-Irvine, found that receiving music lessons from a young age could cause a small but long-lasting increase in IQ. The pleasure derived from music has been shown to actually improve physical health and increase well-being in patients. Lullabies, with their narrow pitch range, triple meter, and soft predictability, can help slow down heart rate and induce sleep. Music therapy has also become a popular rehabilitative tool, helping to relieve pain and reduce stress. 

Perhaps, it’s this combination of relief, pleasure, and intellect that makes music so instrumental in facilitating political change. One thing is clear, though. Music has touched the hearts and has fueled the passions of billions, extending its reach throughout time and space.
