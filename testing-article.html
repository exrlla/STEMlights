Daylight Savings Time

Last week, we sprung ahead one hour for Daylight Savings Time (DST). But how and why did this practice start, and how does it affect our bodies?

It is commonly thought that Benjamin Franklin was the first to propose the concept of Daylight Savings. In a letter Franklin wrote known as “An Economical Project for Diminishing the Cost of Light," he suggested that people could use the extra daylight in the evening to lessen the number of candles they need to burn.

In 1895, New Zealand scientists George Vernon Hudson and British builder William Willett suggested shifting the time ahead two hours in October and back two hours in March. However, the idea was not followed through.

It was not until the 20th century when Daylight Savings was more commonly adopted. In 1916, during World War I, Germany began DST to conserve fuel. Other countries in Europe followed, and it was adopted by the U.S. in 1918.

Though DST became a common practice a little over a hundred years ago, ancient civilizations have had similar time-changing practices that were used up to thousands of years ago. For example, Roman water clocks used different scales and were adjusted to adjust for solar time.

A side effect of this time change often results in increased tiredness the following week as people's bodies adjust. This can cause an increase in traffic accidents and work injuries due to inadequate sleep. Scientists suggest waking up earlier the week prior, eating healthy, and exposing oneself to direct sunlight in order to make the time adjustment slightly easier.

Today, nearly 40% of the world recognizes DST. There has also been increased movement to abolish this practice altogether, and some places like Arizona don’t recognize daylight savings time at all. However, no concrete steps have been taken to get rid of DST, so it seems as though we will be ‘springing ahead’ and ‘falling back’ for many years to come.

 
 
 
 
 

Eye Color More Complicated Than Previously Believed

Genetics control eye color, but a new study found 50 new genes that code for eye color. Researchers previously believed that eye color was coded by two genes, one for blue and one for brown, with brown dominant. These new findings, however, build upon a previous study displaying that eye color is encoded from 12 genes. This research could potentially enhance knowledge on changes in eye pigment, present in albinism.

Plastic Toys May Contain Harmful Chemicals

Plastic, one of the most common materials used in toys, may contain harmful chemicals, including phthalates and brominated flame retardants, potentially causing cancer. Children often place toys into their mouths, which further exacerbates these risks. Since international regulations for chemicals in toys do not exist, the existence of these chemicals are not surprising. However, these chemicals can easily be swapped out for a safer, more sustainable alternative.

Mars Water Is Still Trapped On Mars

Researchers previously knew Mars once contained abundant water across its surface, with oceans and lakes covering the planet, but they have long-believed the water was expelled into space. New research from CalTech now speculates that this water is stored within Mars’s crusts. Atmospheric data seems to align more accurately with this new theory rather than the former theory.

Dr. Susan La Flesche Picotte

Welcome back to History x STEM! Today, we’ll be highlighting Dr. Susan La Flesche Picotte, and her amazing impact as a trailblazer in the medical field. 

La Flesche was born in the Omaha reservation on June 17, 1865. When she was young, La Flesche witnessed a Native American woman pass away after a white doctor never came to her care. In this moment, La Flesche “saw the need of [her] people for a good physician,” and began to work towards studying medicine, graduating second in her class at Hampton University. 

Soon afterwards, La Flesche applied to the Woman’s Medical College of Pennsylvania for medical school, breaking the social stereotypes this heavily white-male-dominated field imposed on women and underrepresented individuals. But despite these challenges, three years later, La Flesche graduated as valedictorian and became the first Native American, in the United States, to earn a medical degree. 

La Flesche went back to the Omaha reservation, and at the age of 24, she became the only physician for over 1,200 patients. Although she resigned from this position by 1894, La Flesche continued to operate a private practice and also opened up a local hospital. In 1909, she even helped Omaha gain the ability to control their land by leading a delegation to Washington D.C., rightfully stating that “the majority of the Omaha are as competent as the same number of white people.”

Dr. Susan La Flesche Picotte dedicated her life to heal others, no matter their race or gender. Through her incredible work as a physician and activist, she has helped improve countless lives and has inspired a multitude of generations.

 

Amberley Martinez

Previously an intern over the summer and then a writer for the Politics x STEM section of the STEMlights newsletter, Amberley Martinez is now the Marketing and Editing coordinator for STEMlights. Based out of University High School in California, Martinez is passionate about pursuing “the intersection of STEM and the humanities, and finding a way to use that crossroads to help others.”

Martinez plans to major in engineering or computer science with a minor in English literature, connecting with others who are interested in the interdisciplinary aspects of STEM. A lifelong lover of reading and philosophy as well as astronomy, space, and engineering, her goal is to “push the boundaries of intersectionality, using technology to help others by promoting interconnectivity, equitable education, and space exploration.” 

Martinez was motivated to get involved in STEMchats to explore this rising interest in intersectionality and step outside of her comfort zone. The STEMchats principles of equitable education have deeply resonated with Martinez. “Education is where we learn to love and hate; it’s an innate human desire to want to care passionately about something, or anything really. With accessible education, we can refocus humanity’s passions into something that will improve us not only technologically, but ethically as well,” she comments. 

Martinez’s plans for the future include “pushing the boundaries of intersectionality, using technology to help others by promoting interconnectivity, equitable education, and space exploration.” Martinez explains that STEMchats has taught her so much that can be applied to her intellectual pursuits, but also her personal life. She says that her time with STEMchats has educated her about “perpetually learning from failure, found community, and self-discovery.” She also describes STEMchats's influence in “not necessarily defining yourself or putting yourself in a box, but finding things to be passionate about, to pursue and finding others to share that interest.”
