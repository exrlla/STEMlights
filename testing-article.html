Jurassic Park: DNA and Cloning

Image from TheCX

Welcome back to another section of “Sci-Fi or Fact”, the series where we break down pop culture and answer your burning pop culture questions. Today, we will be debunking the science behind the famous “Jurassic Park” movie and novel. If you don’t already know, “Jurassic Park” is about a world in which scientists genetically engineered dinosaurs back from extinction. How exactly did these scientists do this? Is this even possible in real life? Let's find out. 

In the movie, scientists collected dinosaur DNA from blood cells of prehistoric mosquitoes. These mosquitos were preserved in an organic substance, formed from tree bark, known as Amber. To learn more about Amber, click here. Can prehistoric mosquitoes even store DNA for millions of years? Yes! In a 2013 discovery, a researcher from the National Museum of History, named Dale Greenwalt, found a prehistoric mosquito preserved in a sedimentary rock known as shale. Greenwalt and his team found organic compounds, porphyrins, and DNA from prehistoric times. Although this DNA was stored for millions of years, researchers were able to identify which organism the DNA belonged to. Greenwalt’s work, along with many other scientific studies, prove that the preservation of DNA (and other complex organic molecules) in mosquitos is scientifically possible. However, the fossilization inside Amber and the ability to identify the specific prehistoric DNA is scientifically inaccurate in the movie.

Now let’s say, hypothetically, scientists were able to extract and identify dinosaur DNA from the mosquito. Is it possible to resurrect and replicate species from extinction?  

Last year, scientists successfully cloned an extinct species known as the black-footed ferret. These ferrets were declared extinct by 1979, due to lack of food, poison, and plagues. However, in 1981, a farmer discovered a family of black-footed ferrets in Wyoming. Scientists took this to their advantage and preserved their DNA. Scientists successfully cloned this species by inserting the preserved DNA in a domestic ferret unfertilized egg! To read more about this amazing discovery, click here. In the end, the idea of cloning extinct species is extremely new, but not entirely impossible. Although the world of “Jurassic Park” may seem futuristic, the science behind it is not too far out of this generation’s reach. 

 
 
 
 

SpaceX’s Inspiration4 Mission

Inspiration4 has returned from space! The SpaceX mission took the civilian crew almost 366 miles above the Earth. That's even higher than the International Space Station — by over 100 miles! It is the first space mission ever to have all civilians onboard. The four astronauts (Jared Isaacman, Hayley Arceneaux, Chris Sembroski, and Dr. Sian Proctor) spent three days aboard the Crew Dragon spacecraft. The Inspiration4 mission as a whole is a large fundraising effort to support St. Jude Children’s Research Hospital. This mission is revolutionary as Dr. Proctor piloted the spacecraft, making her the first Black woman to do so. Arceneaux, at age 29, is also the youngest American to travel to outer space!

 

Why Humans Don’t Have Tails

Have you ever wondered why humans don’t have tails? Recent research has shown that humans and apes have a mutation in the TBXT gene. But, this is a mutation that monkeys and other tailed animals don’t have. The study, conducted by Bo Xia from the NYU Grossman School of Medicine, experimented on mice. Some were mutated to carry the gene while others were not. They found that the ones with TBXT developed either no tail or a very short one. Xia claims that the mutation may have spontaneously occurred in an ape almost 20 million years ago but the true reason remains unknown.

Elephant Trunks

Elephants may be the solution to creating advanced robots! Scientists have been studying elephant trunks, famous for their fluid movement and versatility. Elephant trunks combine only 20 different movements to achieve an almost endless range of motion that allows them to carry light and heavy objects. The trunks can be powerful but also gentle and handle delicate objects. Elephants also use suction power and can wrap their trunks around objects to pick them up. Their trunks can even become like pseudo-joints, similar to a human’s elbow joint. By studying elephant trunks, researchers may be able to improve robotic arms and other prosthetic limbs in the future!

 

Sir Robert Edwards

Photo from ZEISS Microscopy 

Hello STEMlights readers, today we will be looking into the life of Robert Edwards, Ph.D, the major innovator of in-vitro fertilization (IVF) in 1978.

Edwards was born on September 27, 1925, in Yorkshire, England. He joined the British Army, and eventually received a Bachelor’s in Science at Bangor University, and was awarded a Ph.D. in 1955 at the Institute of Animal Genetics and Embryology through the University of Edinburgh.

He began a postdoctorate at CalTech and eventually returned to the British Isles. He became a professor at the University of Glasgow and the University of Cambridge, primarily in physiology.

Around 1960, his research in fertilization began to flourish. He was interested in human fertilization with the need of a womb, and in 1968, he successfully fertilized an egg in a laboratory. Collaborating with gynecologist Patrick Steptoe, they began a nearly ten-year research experiment to bring an IVF-induced pregnancy to term. They received little funding, especially at the beginning of their research. 

By 1977, they had successfully implanted a fertilized egg into patient Lesley Brown. Brown and her husband had struggled to conceive prior to the advent of IVF. Lesly’s child, Louise Brown, was born in July of 1978. She, Steptoe, and Edwards became internationally known for what was thought of as a medical miracle.

As a result of the invention of IVF, Dr. Edwards was awarded the Nobel Prize in Medicine in 2010 and passed away in 2013.

Olin E. Teague Scholarship

The National Space Club and Foundation offers the Olin E. Teague scholarship annually in memory of Olin E. The applicant must plan to pursue undergraduate or graduate studies in an aerospace field AND must have conducted original aerospace research. Applications are due October 15th!
