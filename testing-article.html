COVID-19 Vaccine Inequity

Photo by Daniel Schludi on Unsplash

As more people are becoming eligible for the COVID vaccine in the United States, many other countries are seeing an increase in COVID cases but are maintaining limited access to these vaccines. This brings up a significant dilemma regarding contemporary globalization and inequity within medical access and care.

When the pandemic first started, many people felt a sense of global unity. However, the pandemic has heightened already existing systemic inequalities, now evident in vaccine distribution.

Nearly half of the COVID-19 vaccines have been purchased by wealthy countries. For example, the United States has purchased nearly three times the supply to vaccinate all adults. It will take almost two more years for "poorer" countries to vaccinate all their citizens. Thus, as many people in the West are beginning to see the "light at the end of the tunnel," we must also be cognizant that the same is not true for many other countries.

There are multiple reasons for the vast inequality in access to the COVID vaccine. For one, wealthier countries were able to make deals with pharmaceutical companies and pre-ordered millions of vaccines before their release. This same luxury was not granted to other countries. 

Additionally, issues with patenting and access to vaccine-making materials have become problematic. Some believe pharmaceutical companies should waive the patent, so other companies can begin manufacturing the vaccine. However, suppliers have stated that they have had issues acquiring necessary materials; releasing the patent would not automatically fix the issue.

To streamline the process, countries with an excess supply of the vaccine can donate it to other countries who need it. Moreover, they can also help other countries address the issue firsthand. Regardless, something should be done, and as many rejoice in getting vaccines, we must not forget the global community and their response to COVID.

 
 
 
 

First Flight for Mars Helicopter

Last month marked the first flight for NASA’s Ingenuity Mars Helicopter, the first instance of controlled flight on another planet. On April 19th at 3:34 am Pacific Daylight Time, the helicopter achieved autonomous flight, reaching ten feet (three meters) in altitude and maintaining stability for 30 seconds. Engineers at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory in California constructed the helicopter and flight algorithms, accounting for differences between Mars and Earth, like pressure and gravity. Project manager MiMi Aung described Ingenuity’s historic flight as “our Wright brothers moment on Mars,” and the team hopes to use future tests to improve and innovate upon the current design.

 

Coral Reefs Preventing Floods

In a study conducted by the University of California, Santa Cruz, researchers found that over $1.8 billion in infrastructure -- including roads, hospitals, and power plants as well as homes and communities -- was saved by coral reefs preventing rising sea levels. One meter in reef height equates to $5.3 billion in damages, with 200 miles of reefs off of the United States coast. With reefs in danger due to climate change and commercial efforts, the study provides more economic incentive for policymakers to invest in reef restoration and protection.

Scientists Create Human-Monkey Embryos

Scientists in China and the United States successfully injected human cells into monkey embryos, with the survival rate lasting longer than ever before. Scientists commented that the percentage of human embryos was high throughout the growth period. Amid ethical concerns, scientists pointed out that the experiment allows them to examine cellular and interspecies communication as well as molecular pathways, paving the way for developments in drugs and transferring cells, tissues, and organs.

Human-Induced Extinction Among Animals

Photo by McGill Library on Unsplash

Hello , today we will be discussing human-induced extinction among animal populations. It is essential to conserve what we have left on this Earth, as extinction is an ever-growing contemporary issue.

Arguably, human-induced extinction has occurred since the late Quaternary Period (about 11,700 - 50,000 years ago). Many scientists believe that the late Quaternary period gave rise to the extinction of the Neanderthals, wooly mammoths, and many large mammals, especially around the Last Glacial Period(115,000-11,700 years ago). However, most scientists agree that these extinctions were a mix of human-induced, environmental, and evolutionary factors.

The Holocene Period (11,700 years ago - Present Day) has seen the efforts of mankind give rise to technological advancements in agriculture, transportation, and philosophy. However, warfare, pollution, and colonization have resulted in the imminent destruction of much of this planet’s ecosystems. With the onset of European colonization and the imperialization of much of the world, human activity quickly increased extinction rates. For example, the dodo bird, the Rodrigues solitaire, and the Reunion solitaire were all “discovered” by Europeans in Mauritius, the island of Rodrigues, and the island of Reunion, respectively. Portuguese colonizers, as they began to conquer the three birds’ respective islands from 1507 - the 1600s, over-harvested and inevitably caused the birds to go extinct. The dodo, extinct by 1681 despite having no natural predators, is now a symbol of the importance of preventing human-induced animal extinction. It was killed primarily for its meat for the Portuguese sailors. In the present day, the dodo bird’s remains have been rare excavation treasures, though thousands of Rodrigues solitaire bones have been discovered. In 2016, scientists were able to sequence the dodo genome, so it may be able to undergo de-extinction, or a revival of the species, in the future.

Contemporary extinction has been exacerbated by increased pollution and industrialization of the world: human-induced extinction in the contemporary era. With the inevitable rise of climate change, animal populations are subject to warmer or cooler temperatures than they can handle. It also changes and possibly eliminates hibernation and migration patterns for animals. The International Union for the Conservation of Nature predicts that 67% of currently endangered species will go extinct in the next 100 years. They claim that it will take a daunting 3-5 million years for the Earth to recover in terms of biodiversity as a result of this proclaimed sixth mass extinction in the last 450 million years. Animals such as the Spix’s Macaw, the Black Rhinoceros, and the Sumatran Tiger are rapidly going extinct in the wild and captivity, and intervention must take place to prevent this. 

If you are interested in learning more about preventing mass extinction, feel free to check out the IUCN’s website, https://www.iucn.org/.

New York Academy of Sciences Student Programs

The New York Academy of Sciences is offering not one, but TWO mentoring opportunities for students aged 13-17! The first program, 1000 Girls, 1000 Futures, focuses on connecting girls to female mentors for a year long educational mentorship experience. Those accepted will be able to develop and nurture their skills while being integrated to a wide network of female STEM leaders. The second program, The Junior Academy, takes in passionate and hard working students to grant them access to STEM courses and resources. During the program, mentors and professionals will guide student teams through various challenges as friendly competition and to test out their knowledge and collaborative skills. Applications for both programs will close on July 31st and admission decisions will be sent out in early September.
