The History and Meaning Behind Juneteenth

Juneteenth, also referred to as Emancipation Day, is a holiday celebrating the end of slavery in the United States. It is also known as America’s second Independence Day, Jubilee Day, Juneteenth Independence Day, and Black Independence Day. It is the oldest African American holiday and is celebrated on June 19th every year. This holiday commemorates the day in 1865 when Union troops seized control of Texas and liberated all enslaved people. The troops were led by Major General Gordon Granger, who informed the enslaved African Americans in Galveston, Texas of their freedom. This event occurred two and a half years after former President Abraham Lincoln signed the Emancipation Proclamation and months after the civil war ended. 

At the first celebration of Juneteenth in Texas, many African Americans heard about the Emancipation Proclamation for the first time. This day was initially honored by spiritual singing, prayer gatherings, and the donning of new clothes, which symbolizes freedom. In the span of a few years, Juneteenth was adopted as an annual holiday in other states. Juneteenth almost faded out several times in the past during World War II and the civil rights movement. However, the holiday gained traction again in the 21st century.

Before June 17, 2021, North Dakota, South Dakota, and Hawaii did not officially commemorate the holiday. Today, Juneteenth is honored in the same ways as it was in the past. Prayer and religious activities, addresses, educational activities, social gatherings and picnics, and festivities involving food and music are all common elements of Juneteenth celebrations in the United States. 

In the past, the holiday has been honored by former presidents Barack Obama, Donald Trump, Bill Clinton, George W. Bush. Many organizations, both private and governmental, have taken measures to recognize the occasion. Nike and Twitter are making great strides by compensating their employees on Juneteenth. Outside of the U.S, Juneteenth is observed to appreciate African American culture and immense contributions to society.

On June 17, 2021, President Biden signed the Juneteenth National Independence Day Act into law, making it a national holiday. When President Biden signed the act into law, he made a powerful statement: "...we must understand that Juneteenth represents not only the commemoration of the end of slavery in America more than 150 years ago, but the ongoing work to have to bring true equity and racial justice into American society, which we can do. In short, this day doesn’t just celebrate the past; it calls for action today."

Dr. Daniele Visioni

Dr. Dan Visioni is a postdoctoral researcher and climate modeler at Cornell University in New York. His latest project is called GAUSS (Geoengineering Assessment across Uncertainties, Scenarios and Strategies), a way to inform the public about the effects of stratospheric aerosol injection, a controversial geoengineering approach to the irreversible effects of climate change. With this project, “we aim to systematically evaluate the state of our knowledge on the subject,” said Dr. Visioni.  

Dr. Visioni discovered this specific area of research a bit randomly; he hadn’t heard about it until his PhD advisor proposed the topic. After further exploring, “I found out how deep the topic is: there are plenty of elements of climate modeling, risk management, societal outreach, philosophical questions, and so many chances to collaborate with many people with various expertise,” he explained. Dr. Visioni’s favorite projects, so far, have been his collaborative research projects and papers. Emailing one professor with an inquiry can lead to a chain reaction. Last year, Dr. Visioini worked on, “a [soil biogeochemistry] paper with the help of everybody!”

The job comes with freedom to explore — there’s always something new and exciting going on. It’s 40-40-20, in his words: 40% talking to people, 40% doing his own scientific work, and 20% studying and keeping himself up-to-date. One common misconception about geoengineering is that those who study the science don’t believe in limiting fossil fuels. “We know that there is only one solution to climate change: stop putting more CO2 in our atmosphere,” he explained. “What drives us is not the will to avoid that, nor a kind of hubris that we get to control the climate, but more a genuine worry that whatever we do might not be enough to avoid some of the bad things coming our way.” It's important to understand that, "we have a right to make an informed decision about it.”

Dr. Visioni’s identity as a member of the LGBTIA+ community makes him even prouder to be a researcher changing the world. “Sometimes it can be as simple as always having a pin with the rainbow flag on my lapel,” he said. “I’ve had more than one person come to me because of the pin because they felt like it was safe to talk to me about problems they might be having around their sexuality, and being open about it.”.

He continues to encourage people around him to ask questions and to never be afraid to stand up for yourself, especially when it comes to bullying in academia. “Never nod along if you don’t understand something, both in the class and when discussing career options.” He also hopes to inspire youth to continue pursuing their diverse passions. “Don’t be afraid to look around, explore, and find out what you like doing,” said Dr. Visioni. “There is no silver bullet against climate change: our hope lies in everybody working together.”

Summer Solstice 2021

This year, the Summer Solstice was observed on Sunday, June 20, signifying the beginning of Summer’s season! During the Summer Solstice, the Sun stands directly above the Tropic of Cancer and because the Sun appears to not be moving, the term Solstice is coined from the Latin words sol (Sun) and sistere (standing still). Although the Sun peaks during the Summer Solstice, Earth’s temperature does not reach its highest until approximately six weeks later because the water on our planet takes a longer time to heat and cool due to a higher specific heat.

 

Scoliosis Awareness

Scoliosis is considered an abnormal (sideways) spinal curvature, affecting approximately six to nine million people in the United States (2-3% of the U.S. population). Most cases are diagnosed in the stages of childhood or early adolescence, with symptoms ranging from uneven shoulders to changes in texture of the skin right above the spine. Spinal development, degree of curvature, and the location of where the spine curves are all matters of evaluating the types/necessary treatments for a patient. Treatment of scoliosis includes spinal bracing or even surgery that can prevent the curve from progressing and reduce the deformity.

Butterflies, Moths, and Climate Change

Recent findings suggest that of the very few species of moths and butterflies that have the ability to adapt to changing climatic conditions, most thrive best in the Nordic country of Finland. Because 40% of the remaining Lepidoptera species have a difficult time increasing their flight period to move in the north direction (hence making it arduous to adjust to changes in climate), their populations are declining.

The Physics of the Canary Cry

Black Canary is best known for fighting alongside the Justice League, cleaning the crime-ridden streets in the Arrowverse, and teaming up with other DC superheroines in the DCEU's Birds of Prey. The “silver-tongued songstress” possesses the supersonic “Canary Cry” — a piercing, high-frequency scream powerful enough to propel her forward as she glides across long distances, ultrasonically incapacitating her enemies along the way. According to DC comics, her scream can cause ears to bleed from over 600 miles away, and can also kill listeners. Could this ever occur in real life?

The answer is no. But the concepts behind her power (it's known in general pop culture as the “Sonic Scream,”) are rooted in real-life physics. In fact, physics is used to create real life sonic and ultrasonic weapons, such as the Long Range Acoustic Device.

Black Canary uses her metahuman vocal cords to produce a high frequency sound that can incapacitate foes. When these high frequencies hit a human eardrum the rapid vibrations cause severe damage. Some comics claim she can produce hypersonic sounds (sounds that exceed a thousand million hertz)! Her “Canary Cry” vibrations are also very intense — there is a great amount of energy transported and the waves have high amplitudes.

One of the Black Canary’s defining traits is her unrealistic ability to soar through the air, using her cry as propulsion. Acoustic levitation is indeed a real phenomenon that uses high-intensity ultrasonic sound to counteract gravity. It has been tested on small beads and water droplets. Researchers have even experimented with dynamic control of these hovering objects! As of now, however, there is no way to blast large objects or humans into the air with just sound. Not only would this require incredibly intense sound waves, but we also do not have adequate technology to transform sound into thrust.

In reality, the closest humans we have to Black Canary are the current Guinness World Record holders for “highest notes sung by humans.” Wang Xiaolong can sing at 5243 Hz, far below the ultrasonic hearing threshold of 20,000 Hz. While Black Canary’s metahuman abilities are improbable in the modern world, the physics concepts integrated into her story are relevant as scientists make new discoveries in sound, waves, sonomechanics, and sonochemistry. It also makes us wonder: how would we protect ourselves from large scale ultrasonic attacks? While Black Canary’s (literally) mind blowing superpowers aren’t real, it’s interesting to see superhero stories so grounded in physics. Keep this in mind when you go and watch the next DC movie or Marvel movie coming out in theaters!

For our second installment of inveSTEMgations, we’ve decided to tackle one of the most famous and highly debated conspiracy theories. Was the moon landing real? Now there is a TON of different theories surrounding this historical event. For this chapter of InveSTEMgations, we are only going to focus on the scientific possibilities (or impossibilities) of the 1969 moon landing. 

On July 20, 1969, about half a billion people watched Neil Armstrong say “That’s one small step for a man, one giant leap for mankind”. The moon landing was an incredible feat for science, technology, and history. Just a few years after that fateful day, people began questioning the validity of this event. One of the first things people noticed that lent to the fake moon landing conspiracy is the flag. When Buzz Aldrin and Neil Armstrong planted the American flag on the moon’s surface, it seemed to be waving in the wind. Unfortunately, there is no wind on the moon due to its’ lack of atmosphere. Therefore, how could the flag possibly wave on the moon’s surface? According to Rick Feinburg, the press officer for the American Astronomical Society, the flag ‘waving’ in the wind has a simple engineering explanation. A horizontal rod was inserted into the flag so that it would look more aesthetically pleasing in pictures. Unfortunately, when the flag was planted into the moon’s surface, the astronauts had some trouble fully extending the rod, which accounts for the ripples and waviness appearing on the flag. 

Another issue people found with the moon landing was the different shadows that appeared in the images. How is it physically possible for certain objects to be seen even though they were in shadows? Since the sun is really the only source of illumination in space, there should be no way to view those objects if they were in shadow. Theorists believe that there must be other sources of light in order to see these objects, and therefore, the moon landing must have been conducted on a Hollywood set with different lights. However, the sun is not the only source of light out in space, technically. The moon’s surface also reflects light from the sun. So even though some objects are in shadow, they can still be seen due to the reflection from the moon. 

Overall, there are a ton of other theories that draw suspicion to the validity of the moon landing. Coming from a scientific standpoint though, it seems like everything was real.

Nicole Felix-Tovar

A leading voice, CEO of STEMchats, and aspiring physician Nicole Felix-Tovar’s journey to Emory shows that a lack of resources does not determine the potential for impact. Felix-Tovar attended Gateway High School, an underfunded high school with few STEM opportunities and no external STEM outreach initiatives.

“I mainly searched for communities of students interested in STEM online, and spent a lot of my time watching YouTube videos of people working in STEM (mostly student doctors and healthcare professionals) to get inspired! Through my research about STEM and thanks to my curiosity, I actually came to find out more about actual scientific research. I was really intrigued by the possibility of actually being able to do research, especially as a high school student. I discovered summer research programs for high school students and was lucky enough to be accepted and participate in Emory Winship Cancer Institute’s Summer Scholars Research Program,” Nicole continued to share, “My mentor, Dr. Bradley Carthon, MD, PhD, was incredibly patient and inclusive, my program colleagues and friends were nice and supportive, and I felt myself grow so much personally, academically, and professionally. This was incredibly meaningful and eye-opening to me, and I wish the same for every single student interested in STEM.”

In addition to her personal experiences, as Felix-Tovar took advantage of opportunities to grow, her mission to work towards STEM equity developed. “While I wholeheartedly believe that a critical quality to possess in life is the ability to advocate for one’s self, I also believe that it should not be this difficult for students to find opportunities to explore and develop their interests outside of the classroom,” Felix-Tovar explained. “I am so grateful to organizations such as QuestBridge for bringing together such incredible students from similar socioeconomic backgrounds yet unique experiences and lives. I’ve found a way to help other students whose shoes I have been in, and for that, I’m so grateful. For the past year and some change, it has been so inspiring and empowering to get to know and work alongside my peers, which is what I enjoy the most about STEMchats.”

As Nicole heads STEMchats with a personal goal of creating the types of opportunities that she found impactful, she shares some aspirations she is working towards as a rising sophomore at Emory University, “I am currently majoring in Anthropology and Human Biology on the pre-med track. Felix-Tovar concludes her interview with some advice for aspiring STEM activists or anyone looking to make an impact on an issue they care about. “None of the opportunities I’ve found nor the ones that I’ve helped create through STEMchats I could have done entirely alone. It’s so important to put our heads together to learn and create brighter futures for ourselves and others alongside one another! I encourage you to get together with a group of friends and create initiatives for positive change in your community. Even if you don’t think you could do it, I know you can!”

For this edition of STEMlights, we wanted to help you bridge the intersection between COVID-19 and environmental impacts. In the third segment of investemgations, we've decided to see if Coronavirus is the new Mother Teresa. Throughout the global expansion of the pandemic, we have heard all the news reports regarding the environmental benefits from COVID-19. Today, we will help you investigate the reality. 

Global Carbon Emissions (GCEs) began declining when international governments began enforcing social distancing, business, and overall reform, to limit the spread of the virus. According to National Geographic, daily GCEs declined over 17 percent, in comparison to last year. However, looking at the latter months, it seems that "they are only about 5 percent lower than at the same point in 2019", and businesses, as well as factories, are not yet running at their maximized speeds. Many developing countries, such as China and India, began experiencing a decrease in air pollution and particulate matter in the early months of the pandemic. Today, countries like the United States, pose a more substantial risk to the environment. Due to the lack of regulation to limit COVID-19, the paucity of social-distancing in highly-exposed areas, which has led to a foreseeable decrease in environmental health. The lack of legislation controlling air and water pollution regulations, can contribute to the COVID-19 deaths in the United States, and in the world. 

Other research from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, have determined that various areas are benefitting from the pandemic. "Many studies show natural Climate solutions", which leads many researchers to learn about the importance of coronavirus concerning global environmental health. More-so, these researchers are also acknowledging that many global communities, such as rural tropical areas, are not recovering in both health and environmental-terms. 

In essence, the conclusion is that there really is none. More research and future improvements and worsening in health and governmental regulation will determine environmental outcomes. If global communities work together, the true correlation between coronavirus and the environment will be uncovered.

A Fine Line Between Appreciation and Appropriation

Cultural appropriation is the [sub]concious act of adopting another person’s culture. As a negative form of cultural learning, many celebrities, tiktokers, and even historical “icons” have been labeled as appropriators. Whether it be the adoption of a culture’s fashion or design -- sexualizing, selling, and taking over a culture -- is a close-minded practice exhibited by people around the world. 

Using cultural items (jewelry, clothing) with any form of cultural significance and meaning, as a fashion statement, is cultural appropriation. The Kimono Fashion-Trend is a perfect example of a cultural symbol adapted to a fashion-statement and corporate greed. As profits on this “trend” increased with buyers worldwide, people started realizing that wearing and buying the kimono as a profitable item, and not for its cultural symbolism, is a form of cultural appropriation. It’s the subtle tokens in life that strictly can be identified as cultural appropriation. But, what if you taught yourself about Japanese Culture, heard the stories of Japenese-Americans, or took part in cultural exchange? This is where we can start drawing a line. 

Cultural Appreciation is defined as the literal acceptance and appreciation of another person’s culture. Let’s look at this from the perspective of the Kimono Fashion Trend. If you learned about the cultural and historical significance of the Japanese Kimono, whether it be its vitalism during World War II or educating yourself on the importance of this item with Japanese designers, you have drawn the fine line between appreciation and appropriation. However, profiting off of “modernized” Kimonos or using this item as a fashion-statement, does to exemplify cultural appreciation. 

Don’t forget, Cultural Appropriation happens in the STEM-field too! From research in rural areas to aid in disadvantaged communities, a majority of STEM advancements rely on acceptance and cooperation with new cultures. Becoming an open-minded individual and educating yourself on the vast history of those cultures is vital to the future of STEM. It is essential to ensure the people and yourself that you are not taking advantage of one’s culture, overlooking their insight, or establishing cultural dominance and superiority. A common example is the building of a highly-recommended STEM facility on top of sacred grounds in one’s culture. This is not only complete disrespect but the failure of human nature, to recognize the importance of cultural recognition. Not working with cultural groups in rural or urban areas, to facilitate the growth of STEM, will not lead to new technological advances. Instead, we will see a decrease in the diversity within the STEM community and the increased support of a discriminatory system. As the future youth of STEM, we need to integrate the value of cultural knowledge. It remains unseen due to the vast emphasis on technology and lack of focus on cultural appreciation.  

To appropriate is to take, to draw away from meaning, and controversially adopt. To appreciate a culture is to listen, accept, share, and examine. It is our role, as youth leaders, to take the next step and promote appreciation, while systemically denying appropriation and superiority.

 
 
 

Sasha Kolesnikov

As one of STEMchats’ VPs of Business Development, Sasha Kolesnikov’s role is as interdisciplinary as her accomplishments and aspirations. Kolesnikov explores the intersection between science and society at the University of California, Berkeley, and developed an earlier interest in neuroscience and computer science at Pacific Collegiate School. 

Kolesnikov played a crucial role in the development of STEMchats. “I ideated and founded the Business Development Department at STEMchats (which includes the Internal Development, External Development, Funding, Marketing, and Technology teams) and have since focused my efforts on Funding and Internal and External Development and being the acting External Development Lead. I essentially help with all STEMchats processes that support our programs, from raising funds to ensuring every member has a fulfilling and productive experience,” explained Kolesnikov.

The obstacles Kolesnikov overcame and observed while pursuing her academic interests lead to a personal mission that echoes STEMchats’: to shatter barriers in science, technology, engineering, & mathematics (STEM). “I’ve seen what even a little support can do, so I hope to contribute to organizations like STEMchats to help those from my (or other marginalized) backgrounds!” shared  Kolesnikov. “I want to help make these fields and conversations more accessible for people from low-income or disadvantaged backgrounds. For example, though I do think larger systems and corporations are to blame for our current climate crisis, I want to de-gentrify the sustainability space and help support voices of color and low-income individuals in the space.”

Beyond STEMchats, Kolesnikov continues to explore her various interdisciplinary interests while making an impact. “Though I used to be super interested in neuroscience and computer science, I’m honestly not a hardcore STEM person and am more interested in exploring how science and society interact (science communication, sustainability, and environmental economics, data analytics, etc.)” Kolesnikov said. “ I’m also starting a sustainable and ethical brand/collective/zine with my friend that will involve sustainable material sourcing and tons of research on issues surrounding our environmental and ethical issues.”

In addition to adjusting her academic and co-curricular pursuits at UC Berkeley, Kolesnikov is looking forward to continuing to make an interdisciplinary impact. “With STEMchats, I hope to support our amazing team by ramping up fundraising and building meaningful partnerships before eventually passing the torch to another generation of FGLI students! Outside of STEMchats, I hope to facilitate a genuine and realistic conversation that can lead to change. I’ve taken a step back from the heavy business pre-professional track I felt pressure to pursue at Berkeley and instead hope to create a sustainable (both environmentally and in a business sense) small business with people I care about that not only promotes ethical values but also gives opportunity to underrepresented people.” 

 
 

The Rapid Warming of the South Pole                                                                      Scientists from the University of Ohio found that the South Pole is warming at a rate three times the average as a result of natural climate patterns and increased greenhouse gases. While a lot of the warming is linked with the natural warming mechanism of the tropics, the large gap in temperature is the result of human cause. With an alarming warming rate, the South Pole could be in danger of complete annihilation.

Children Show Differences in Gene Regulation Based on Background                          Duke University scientists studied 2,000 children for over 18 years from different communities to explore the difference in epigenetics and gene regulation. They found that children from disadvantaged communities were more likely to have an epigenetic difference in the genes related to inflammation, smoking, pollution, and lung cancer. These studies reflect a long-term socioeconomic disparity that may translate negatively into health.

Plato’s Cube Theory is Accurate                                                                                           In the 5th century BCE, the Greek philosopher Plato posited that all things on Earth are made up of cubes. A team from the University of Pennsylvania explored deeper into this theory and discovered that Plato was in fact correct: the average form of a rock is a cube. After researching the different fragmentation patterns and shapes of a special database of rocks, the theory proved to be largely correct, with the exception of basalt rocks from North Ireland’s Giant’s Causeway due to its unusual cooling process.
