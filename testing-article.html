The Psychology Behind Conspiracy Theories

From the flat earth theory to the moon landing hoax, it seems as though there has been an overall increase in conspiracy theories within the past couple years. But what has caused this increased skepticism, and what is the psychology behind conspiracy theorists?

Some people’s personality characteristics, such as openness to experience, distrust, low agreeability, and Machiavellianism, make one more likely to believe in conspiracies. On the other hand, conspiracy theories also increase after great tragedies and events, creating an almost scapegoat mentality where people try to push blame on someone or something even if it is illogical.

It is also important to note that conspiracy theories exist on a spectrum in which some are much more harmful than others. For example, the popularization of anti-vaxxers and climate change deniers has led many individuals to lose their trust in science as a result of misinformation. Conspiracy theories thrive off of confirmation bias -- “the tendency to reinforce pre-existing beliefs when we look for information” -- which has only been exacerbated in the digital age where there is limitless access to any and all information. Scientists and professionals must now focus their efforts on regaining the trust of certain individuals while also being mindful of how they explain information to the general public.

Furthermore, “studies have also revealed that half of the US population believes in at least one political or medical conspiracy theory. So belief in conspiracy theories is far more “normal” than many of us might think.”

So though conspiracy theories are fun to entertain, it is crucial to be mindful of the information you are consuming and to always have multiple sources of factual information.

 
 
 
 
 
 

Vijeta Revankar

Coming away from her research experiences with an appreciation for impactful STEM opportunities, Vijeta Revankar looks to make an impact in the field of medicine and beyond. Revankar is a member of the i-STEM Scholars team who attends the University of Houston as a Business major on the pre-med track. 

Revankar joined STEMchats to help students access impactful STEM opportunities. “Although it is not involved with my major, STEMchats’ initiative is involved with my track,” Revankar explained. “Being a pre-med is hard, not only because of the challenging classes, but because there is an immense pressure to get yourself involved in areas that you’ve never had the opportunity to previously explore. Knowing how important my research experience was for me in shaping my future plans, I realized that I wanted to afford other students the same opportunities that I had.”

Revankar’s experience conducting research also allowed her to further appreciate the importance of impactful STEM opportunities. “I’ve participated in several STEM initiatives, but the ones that impacted me the most had to do with research. I spent two summers working with the graduate students and professors at UTMB on a bioimaging project, and eventually presented two posters on our research. I also worked for one year under a NASA mentor where I developed my own procedure for quality-controlling engineered tissue,” Revankar noted.  

Revankar’s STEM experiences informed her mission. “My mission has always been to make education more accessible to underprivileged students. I’ve led initiatives in areas such as robotics and coding that have sought to increase the number of kids interested in pursuing a career in the STEM field,” Revankar said.

“I found out about STEMchats last year when I was looking for STEM related organizations that I could involve myself with. I was really excited to hear about STEMchats’ emphasis on serving underprivileged students, and couldn’t believe my luck when applications opened that summer.” Revankar is currently working on i-STEM Scholars’ research program. “Right now, I’m communicating with professors at universities in Texas to see if they would be interested in potentially joining our summer research program as mentors for our students,” Revankar shared.

In the future,  Revankar aspires to attend medical high school and assist in the expansion of STEMchats and the i-STEM Scholars program.

 
 

Hurricanes are more dangerous than before 

Hurricanes are now moving closer inland and weakening slower due to climate change, increasing the amount of damage inflicted on average. A rise in climate change equates to warmer oceans, which increases the amount of moisture in the hurricanes, which allows them to stay for a longer period of time and cause more damage. More inland communities, who do not typically have hurricanes inland, must prepare for the possibility of damage now.

Half of the stars similar to the Sun could have rocky planets with water

A new study with NASA’s Kepler telescope data found that around 50% of stars similar to the sun could have rocky planets with the capacity for water and life. While the most cautious estimates round the number to be at around 7%, there is still a large capacity for life within these planets, making the opportunity for life more exciting.

“Volume control knob” in the brain affecting learning and memory

Dartmouth College researchers discovered a new part of the brain controlling memory and learning. There is a molecular system within the brain that controls the extent to which the electrical signals flow across synapses between neurons, which oversees learning and memory and implicates further connections with neurological diseases such as Alzheimer’s and Parkinson’s disease.
