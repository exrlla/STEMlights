College Admissions During COVID-19

Photo by Vasily Koloda on Unsplash

Each year, colleges have become increasingly more selective with admissions. Many students spend sleepless nights putting their mental health at risk for the mere possibility of an acceptance to a ‘top’ university in the United States. This year's admissions cycle was no different, and with the combination of a rise in applicants and the obstacles posed by the pandemic, acceptance rates plummeted. 

Many top colleges experienced a dramatic rise in applications this year. For example, Harvard maintained a 42% increase in the applicant pool. Most speculate that this is a result of colleges going test-optional for this year's admission cycle as standardized testing became less accessible during the pandemic. Of course, with more applicants and the same number of spots, the chances of an acceptance dwindles. Many colleges had to rely more heavily on grades and extracurriculars than in prior years, altering the expectations for admissions altogether. 

At the same time, however, according to the Common App, many smaller and less competitive colleges saw a decrease in their applicant pool. Additionally, first-generation and low-income students were not applying to as many colleges as seen in prior years. This is most likely due to the lack of financial stability felt by individuals in the middle and lower classes due to COVID-19. Many were not willing to spend as much money on a private university as they were before.

Because of the uncertainty caused by COVID, many colleges do not know how to accurately estimate their yield this year. This has caused speculation that waitlists may be utilized more this year for many schools.

In addition, many people have expressed concern that those who deferred last year have impacted the chances of admission for the class of 2025. Though this may be true at some schools, others such as Yale committed to accepting the same number of applicants as previous years. 

All in all, college admissions this year were harder statistically, but at the same time, many admissions officers became more forgiving to some applicants due to hardships caused by the pandemic. Regardless, it is always important to remember that college decisions are not a reflection of one’s merit or potential for future achievements.

 
 
 
 

Dogs Can Sniff Out Coronavirus

Researchers at the University of Pennsylvania discovered that dogs can sniff out COVID-19 in certain samples, if given the proper training in identifying a specific SARS-CoV-2 odor. These dogs even maintained a 96% accuracy rate on average in uncovering positive COVID-19 tests. Although this detection method isn’t perfect, these findings have shed light on the positive implications of using dogs as community health diagnostic tools.

 

5,000+ Tons of Extraterrestrial Dust Fall on Earth Annually

Dust particles from comets or asteroids, called micrometeorites, often fall on Earth. However, a group of scientists found that about 5,200 tons of micrometeorites reach the ground on Earth per year. For the last two decades, these scientists initiated six expeditions near Antarctica to collect space dust particles, measuring their annual flux to calculate how many tons of micrometeorites reach Earth every year. Through their work, we can further understand how these micrometeorites helped supply water and necessary nutrients during the establishment of life on Earth.

Sugar Can Negatively Affect Brain Development

Although eating sugar may seem tasty and fun, a study by the University of Georgia suggests that daily consumption of sugar (in beverages) during childhood could negatively impact learning and memory as an adult. The researchers discovered that, in animals, an increase in sugar could cause an increase in bacteria called Parabacteroides within the gut, leading to poorer performance in task-related experiments. Thus, the Dietary Guidelines for Americans recommends consuming sugar as less than 10% of one’s total calories per day.

Glenn T. Seaborg

"Glenn T. Seaborg - Seaborgium" by Berkeley Lab is licensed under CC BY-NC-ND 2.0

Happy Monday! Today in STEM history, Glenn T. Seaborg was born 109 years ago. Let’s discuss his life, work, and legacy regarding the periodic table.

Glenn T. Seaborg was an American chemist born in April 1912. Born in Michigan, his family moved to California in 1922, where Seaborg graduated from David Star Jordan High School in Los Angeles as the valedictorian of the Class of 1929. He went to college at UCLA and UC Berkeley, earning him a Ph.D. in chemistry in 1937. Appointed as a professor of chemistry at the latter, Seaborg dedicated himself to research at the Lawrence Radioactive Laboratory, the General Advisory Committee, and the Atomic Energy Commission, much of his research occurring with the Second World War as the backdrop. He was also the Chancellor of the University of California at Berkeley from 1958 to 1961. 

During his tenure as a professor, he began research on radiochemical science. His collaboration with physicist Jack Livingood led to the discovery of Iodine-131, today an incredibly necessary radioactive thyroid gland treatment. It even later saved his own mother’s life. In 1940, Edwin McMillan of UC Berkeley isolated the first transuranium (elements beyond Uranium on the Periodic Table) element with the synthesis of neptunium. By 1941, Seaborg synthesized and identified plutonium, element 94, as the next transuranium element. Although plutonium is found in nature, never before had it been discovered scientifically. By 1942, he had synthesized Plutonium fluoride. This synthesis of plutonium was then taken by Seaborg and other chemists and physicists to develop the destructive atomic bombs, essentially a macro-scale version of the plutonium synthesis. 

Seaborg also discovered and synthesized elements 95 and 96 by 1944, and further discovered 97 - 102, meaning that he discovered elements from plutonium to nobelium. He also was the first to identify Seaborgium, an element named in his honor before his death (106 on the periodic table). Seaborg's discoveries were the most significant change in the periodic table by one person since Dmitri Mendeleev, the inventor. 

Glenn Seaborg passed away in February 1999, at the age of 86. His contributions to chemistry and nuclear physics were nothing but astounding.
