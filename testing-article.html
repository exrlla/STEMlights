Surge of COVID-19 Cases in India

India’s population comprises 18% of the world’s population. As a result, when the pandemic began, containing COVID-19 in such a densely populated country proved difficult. After getting over the first wave of COVID-cases, the Prime Minister released restrictions in the country. Following this decision, thousands of people flocked to events in massive numbers, and in one case, a crowd of supporters can be seen cheering for Modi in preparation for a state election. 

After the incident, India has been grappling with record-high cases. For one, the country has recorded over 261,000 new cases, which is more than what most countries have recorded since the beginning of the pandemic. For the past two weeks, the cases have exceeded 300,000, half of the number of global cases reported on a day-to-day basis. On Friday, May 7, India reported more than 414,000 new cases, an unheard of number. Experts fear that the number of cases may be much higher than what has been recorded.

Many of the citizens in India are blaming Prime Minister Modi for celebrating the country’s victory over the first wave prematurely, causing the surge in cases the country is currently experiencing. As of now, the death toll in the country in a single day has surpassed 4,000 people. The capital city has exhausted its supply of wood, typically used for the cremation process. 

In a strained healthcare system, devastated families are struggling to keep themselves and their loved ones healthy. Hospitals in India are receiving an influx of patients every day, and ventilators, ICU equipment, and other medical supplies are out of stock. Although the country is the biggest producer of COVID-19 vaccines, the supply of vaccines is running low. 

The country has purchased 205.5 million doses of COVID-19 vaccines, making India among the top ten vaccine buyers in the world. However, those shots can only reach 8% of the country's 1.4 billion population. As the crisis in India worsens, countries including the United States, United Kingdom, France, and Germany are sending monetary aid and personal protective equipment (PPE) to India to alleviate the effects of the pandemic. Citizens are volunteering to assist overcrowded clinics.

Mental Health Issues in Higher Education

Photo by Good Free Photos on Unsplash

During this Mental Health Awareness Month, we discuss mental health with not one, not two, but thousands of other students both in and out of STEM who are struggling with mental health. While many can attribute the usual mood swing to a change in hormones or a particularly bad day, long-term and serious mental health issues can be debilitating to living a happy and content life.

Whether it be depression or anxiety from a high-pressure environment, students and professionals alike can find it difficult in speaking out about the impact that stress can have on their life. Speaking out and seeking professional help is a life-saving practice that is often washed under the waves of stigma surrounding mental health.

In a study conducted in 2020, graduate students were found to have 6 times the chance of becoming depressed compared to the norm. Studies have indicated that every 34 in 100 female engineering students suffer from some form of mental health issue. In a study of 31 institutions, the weed-out introductory chemistry course had a 29.41% drop-out or fail rate, 40% of which were Latinx or Black. Other mechanisms simply embedded in society such as microaggressions and harassment have made their mark on disproportionate levels of women and minorities reporting depression, anxiety, or panic attacks. The idea of “publish or perish,” a term coined in 1932, continues to this day in research facilities to publish large and influential research for their field.

There is a significant challenge in confronting these mental health concerns. Fortunately, educating others to be able to recognize the needs of their peers and colleagues as well as providing support is slowly becoming a norm. Layers of stigma surrounding the discussion of poor mental health and highly competitive environments in classrooms and workplaces alike have started to gain more attention. 

 
 
 
 

Bats and Speed of Sound

A study conducted through Tel Aviv University suggests that bats acquire the knowledge of the speed of sound from birth. To demonstrate these scientific claims, researchers gathered and raised a group of bats from conception in an environment with a higher than usual speed of sound (helium-enriched setting). Following this experiment, researchers concluded that bats perceive the world through units of time. This means that rather than perceiving an insect as being distanced one and a half meters away from it, bats see the insect as nine milliseconds away.

Mental health issues are fairly common in American society, with one in five Americans experiencing some sort of mental health problem. Early signs of mental health issues, including mood swings or persistent tiredness, can even be seen in children, with causing factors ranging from biological, psychological, and social problems. The Month of the Strawberry

Strawberries are one of the first fruits to ripen in the Spring, with preferred temperatures ranging from 55 to 78 degrees Fahrenheit. On average, each strawberry contains around 200 seeds, with about five to seven petals on each piece of sweet, juicy fruit. Strawberries are the only fruit that grows their seeds on the outside, compared to other berries, and the sweetness of the fruit is determined by various factors, including ripeness, weather, and variety.

Supertaskers, Do They Exist?

Photo by SerenaWong on Pixabay

Multitasking is a hot topic. Most of us, at one point, have wished to do two or more things concurrently to save some sweet time later for relaxation, but the truth is most of us cannot. Cognitive scientists strive to reveal whether this trait is possible in human beings.

In humans, multitasking refers to performing two or more tasks simultaneously, while the more novel term "supertasking" refers to executing two or more tasks concurrently without any perceivable decline in cognitive performance. According to a team of scientists, heavy media multitasking and attention lapses are directly related; essentially, they showed that there were lapses in attention before the act of remembering and behavioral forgetting for persons who engaged in social media multitasking. 

Supertaskers, though, do not seem to suffer these negative effects of multitasking. The word "supertasker," coined by psychologist Strayer from the University of Utah, refers to persons who can perform complex multitasking with little or no discernible costs in cognitive abilities. With looming reports that multitasking is not as efficient as it seems, it would be interesting to determine whether (among the broad range of those who claim to be multitaskers) there are "supertaskers" who engage in multiple tasks without any discernible loss in performance. In 2010, Watson & Strayer from the University of Utah sought to investigate whether these individuals exist.

Their findings suggest that there are indeed supertaskers in our midst, albeit a small percentage. So supertaskers do exist.

If supertaskers do exist, why are they such a small portion of the population?  Evolutionary hypotheses have been postulated as to why supertaskers are not so ubiquitous. This "supertasking" trait may have few benefits in the modern world, and therefore, has yet to propagate through the population. For now, at least, there may be few benefits related to being a supertasker. Arguably, as the world gets more technologically and professionally demanding, more and more supertaskers may emerge to deal with these demands. Soon, many might concurrently multitask without a decrease in performance or cognitive ability; perhaps study for a midterm while playing the piano.

In a world where attention is a "scarce" commodity, it may be comforting to know that some people possess this commodity in abundance.
