Why are Scary Movies, Scary? 

If you’ve ever watched a scary movie, you’re familiar with the classic horror movie music and sounds that add to the overall experience. One could even argue that horror movies would be a lot less scary if it weren’t for the music. But what makes the music particularly frightening?

UCLA professor of Ecology and Evolutionary Biology, Daniel Blumstein, studied the distress calls of marmots and noticed the nonlinear noise patterns. He then compared these distress signals to that of other animals and found striking similarities.

Blumstein continues this research and in a study that “explored the link between nonlinear noise and scary music [...] He discovered that horror scores made heavy use of such sounds, and, in films like The Shining, even used recordings of animal screams” These nonlinear sounds trigger and almost fight or flight response within humans and other animals as we’re biologically predispositioned to interpret them as distress signals, making us feel like we’re in danger.

Though this serves as one explanation behind the science of scary music, another reason may be that through societal norms,  “we have learned to associate certain music as “scary” simply because it’s often used in a certain way in films.” Whether it’s biology, society, or both, understanding why music is scary goes a lot farther than what initially meets the eye (or ear).  

 
 
 
 
 
 

Ana Cucalon

A sophomore at the University of Chicago Laboratory Schools, Ana Sofia Cucalon is the co-founder and co-executive director of ATHENA by WiSTEM, alongside Nora Sun. ATHENA’s mission is to grant widely accessible STEM opportunities for high-school women from disadvantaged backgrounds.

In her freshman year, she founded the branch of WiSTEM after reaching out to the then-senior founder at her high school. Cucalon was connected with Nora Sun because of their shared interest in helping minority high-school girls (much like herself) achieve their career goals. 

She believes that her decision to join WiSTEM was a subconscious product of watching her immigrant-mother work in the medical field.  “For the past five years, I have seen the struggles that she has faced to be accepted into the American medical field. I watched as she worked hard to push past the “red flags” that came with being a woman of color who earned her med school degree in Colombia,” she told STEMlights.

ATHENA offers a variety of free educational opportunities including webinars, research camps, and mentorship programs. On social media, they share weekly articles and sources about a slew of related topics. Their efforts have certainly gained traction— over 600 participants tuned in to ATHENA’s Research Webinar, which featured a Nobel Prize-winning speaker. 

Each ATHENA program is artfully named to fit the greek mythology theme: Camp Talaria, Camp Xenia, and Camp Themyscira. Nora Sun selected the name “ATHENA” due to, “the aesthetic pleasure of the short but elegant word, and also the Greek goddess of wisdom whom it represented,” Cucalon explained. “Because of the universality of Greek myth in the modern-day world, she hopes that the sound of the name ATHENA can remind girls of their power and capability.”

Cucalon is an outreach coordinator for WiSTEM and a member of Model UN, as she is also passionate about international relations and law. She’s working on developing new ATHENA programming including blog posts, a magazine, and a college-themed podcast. 

“The thought behind this mission was based on Nora and I's understanding that factors beyond gender also negatively affect women’s capability of pursuing their dream career. This led us to create a program that offers the same opportunities as WiSTEM in a personalized fashion, that is aimed for those who may not have the same access as other women.”

 Learn more about Ana and ATHENA: @athenabywistem, athenabywistem.org, athenabywistem@gmail.com

 
 

How random choices become preferences                                                                             In a study where babies have a choice between two brightly colored blocks, they will most likely never choose the block they did not choose initially when given a choice later. This indicates that babies, not just adults, subconsciously have a preference. According to researchers at Johns Hopkins University, people decide to make a choice a preference after the fact they subconsciously make a choice. 

The discovery of the Hepatitis C virus                                                                               The 2020 Novel Prize in Medicine was awarded to scientists Harvey J. Alter, Michael Houghton, and Charles M. Rice for their discovery of the Hepatitis C virus, a blood-borne viral disease that can lead to cirrhosis and liver cancer. The discovery of the Hepatitis C virus answers questions that were previously unsolved and helps develop antiviral drugs directed towards the fight against Hepatitis C. 

Rain moves mountains                                                                                                          It is commonly known erosion can shape mountains, but new research by the University of Bristol dug deeper into the impact of rain on erosion through scientific models. It is now known rainfall has a larger impact on erosion than previously thought. This new knowledge helps account for rainfall and erosion rates when making models or explanations.
