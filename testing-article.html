The Aftermath of the 2020 Election

Election night this past November 3rd was one for the history books. In the days leading up to it, 100 million Americans had already voted early, or through mail-in ballots, Biden had steady leads in the polls, and both candidates came out with confident rhetoric that they would each be the victor. But any hopes of a regular election went out the window with swing states flipping, claims of fraud and lawsuits mounting, and days of counting ballots with no end in sight. We’re going to walk through the aftermath of the election to address these issues and the result.

The night of November 3rd, most states that were being called were expected: New York, California, and Colorado, all major Democratic strongholds that stayed blue, while deep south states like Alabama, Arkansas, and Mississippi were red. What was most surprising at the beginning of the night was that both Florida and Texas—key states for Trump if he wanted to stand a chance—were initially leading blue. However, as the night progressed, Florida flipped to red due to massive Trump support among the Cuban American community, responding to Trump’s rhetoric regarding the “socialist agenda” should Biden be elected. Texas also eventually flipped red despite massive turnout among Democrats, especially in major cities like Dallas, Austin, and Houston. By the end of the night, two things were made clear: the rural-urban divide in America has never been more substantial, with most large cities voting blue and most rural counties voting red, and the election would run far past election day, with multiple states still counting votes. At the end of the night, Biden and Trump each made statements, with Biden telling Americans to “be patient” and wait for mail-in ballots to be counted, which have been overwhelmingly Democratic. (Wisconsin, Michigan, and Pennsylvania all counted mail-in ballots last.) In contrast, Trump stated that “we did win this election” straight off the bat, citing wins in states that still haven’t been called one way or the other. He also went on to say that Democrats were trying to “steal” the election through voter fraud and he wanted to “stop the count” of allegedly illegal ballots; the President cited no substantial evidence for these claims. Americans went to sleep in the early hours of November 4th, witnessing Trump leading in Wisconsin, Michigan, Pennsylvania, North Carolina, and Georgia by large margins and Biden narrowly leading in Nevada. 

On November 4th, however, Americans woke up to find that both Wisconsin and Michigan flipped blue and have since been called in Biden’s favor. That narrowed the key battleground states to Pennsylvania, North Carolina, Georgia (all Trump-led), and Nevada (led by Biden.) Over the course of the next few days, votes would continue to be counted, albeit slowly and amid difficulties; this delay has induced widespread stress and anxiety amongst Americans, who have taken to the internet to constantly refresh, update, complain, and praise. Then, in the early morning of November 6th, Georgia began leading blue, followed by Pennsylvania. Many Democrats have praised Stacey Abrams, a former representative and current activist who registered 800,000 voters in Georgia, for the current lead Biden has in the state that may earn him the presidency. As projected by AP News on Friday night, Biden had 264 electoral votes, and Trump had 214, Biden needing only one more state to win the electoral college and become the president-elect. Trump has been reacting with lawsuits and demanding recounts, as well as tweeting with claims of fraud in states like Michigan, where allegedly 153,000 ballots were found all voting for Biden, the exact number he needed to win. However, that number ended up being an error, and only 15,300 ballots were found, not enough to give Biden the lead. Twitter has had to fact check 15 of Trump’s 44 tweets since election day, flagging them for misinformation. 

And then, at 11:25 am Eastern Time on Saturday, November 7th, the fifth day of election coverage, Joe Biden won Nevada and Pennsylvania, and the Associated Press called the presidential race, forecasting Joe Biden as the president-elect and soon to be the 46th President of the United States. Not only did Biden make history with over 75 million votes and counting, the most in American history, but Kamala Harris made history as well as the first woman, Black American, and person of South Asian descent to serve as the Vice President. Both Biden and Harris will have to lead the nation through unrequited divisiveness, a pandemic, economic crisis, and whatever else 2020 decides to throw at us, but change is upon us, and it’s a new chapter for America. 

 
 
 
 
 
 

Yasmine Mabene

A first year student at Stanford University, 18-year-old Yasmine Mabele strives to bring change to her community through advocacy and mobilization.

She is most passionate about gun violence prevention, environmental justice, and racial justice, which certainly reflects in her work. Yasmine is the State Director of March for Our Lives California, social media director for Earth Uprising, and serves on the Political Action Committee at Stanford’s Black Student Union. 

In the past year, Yasmine worked with other organizers with the goal of getting an ethnic studies bill passed. “This was important to me in order to diversify our education and to give an accurate narrative in our public schools of our shared history, including our faults such as racism, colonialism, and imperialism. Although this bill did not end up passing, we were able to mobilize hundreds of youth and get them engaged in the fight long term,” she told STEMlights. 

A firm believer in youth voice and civic engagement, she has seen first-hand what happens when youth come together. “We’ve been showing up and getting involved, holding our institutions and our peers accountable.”

In the future, she hopes to see more strides taken towards achieving racial justice and equity in society. This includes “divestment from prisons and policing and investments in education, healthcare, and housing,” she explained. While she hasn’t decided her major at Stanford, she is exploring public health research, specifically related to racial disparities in health. 

Being active on campus and local organizations is a great way for youth to get involved, she advised. “Nothing you are able to offer will be too small. See how your skills and talents can fit into fighting for a larger goal.”

Aside from her organizational work, Yasmine previously worked with former Representative Gabby Giffords on gun violence prevention. As an alumnus of the John Hopkins Youth Institute for Gun Policy and Research, she often leads workshops for the program.

“Organizing has taught me to have perseverance because change takes a long time, and sometimes you’ll never see it. But it’s also shown me how much power one person has and if enough people can come together a fight for an issue they care about, even if they’re not the ones who will see the change, it makes all the difference.”

 
 

Developments in the Discovery of Hidden Matter in our Universe

Galaxies are spread out in the wide universe through networks known as cosmic webs. The filaments that connect these networks are considered as the main storage for most of the ordinary, universal matter (in the form of a diffused hot gas). However, the signal emitted from 40-50% of this gas’ particles goes almost unnoticeable. Recently, statistical analysis revealed these undetectable particles (baryons) through an X-ray emission based on the stacked X-ray signal. This finding opens up the potential for more studies regarding the development of gas in the structural cosmic web.

Great Barrier Reef: Largest Living Organism

The Great Barrier Reef is known to be the largest living structure on Earth, located in the Coral Sea of Queensland, Australia. It extends a distance of 1,429 miles, making up an area of 133,000 square miles. The reef is composed of around 3,000 smaller reefs and is considered to be a marine protected area.

Water Detection on the Moon

NASA's Stratospheric Observatory for Infrared Astronomy (SOFIA) has detected water on the surface of the moonlit by the sun. Although the amount of water confirmed is 100 times less than the amount of water in the entire Saharan desert, it is still a significant amount that has led to tremendous breakthroughs. Several forces are thought to serve as a reason for this water discovery, including the force of micrometeorites or the delivery of hydrogen to the lunar surface powered by the Sun’s solar wind. 

 

Welcome to this edition’s Sci-fi or fact where we’ll be discussing the impact of pop culture on protests all around the world!

There’s no doubt that pop culture is instrumental in influencing mass social change. Just in the U.S., American pop culture phenomena, ranging from Tik Tok to the titular movie, Princess Bride, has stirred up much political momentum. In the days leading up to President Trump’s Tulsa rally held back in June, many Tik Tok users and K-pop fans mobilized to reserve thousands of fake tickets. Although there were mixed reactions on whether or not this mobilization was effective in impacting rally attendance, it was clear that pop culture was still not a force to be reckoned with. This past September, a script reading of The Princess Bride for the Democratic Party of Wisconsin raised about $4.25 million, which, according to Ben Wikler, the chair of the respective organization, was their “biggest fundraiser of any kind, ever.” 

Pop culture has also fueled and served as prominent symbols for the protests in Hong Kong and Thailand. Last year, protests began in Hong Kong to oppose the government’s controversial bill, allowing the extradition of fugitives to mainland China. The people of Hong Kong began peacefully marching to promote democracy, but unfortunately, these marches led to violent conflicts with the government, with protesters accusing the police of brutality and willful ignorance. Unexpectedly, one of the core songs in the 1987 Broadway musical Les Misérables, “Do You Hear the People Sing?” became an anthem of this movement. With its sweeping references to the French Revolution, this song gained a spotlight after clips of protesters singing in an airport circulated.

Similarly, in Thailand, many have recently taken to the streets to protest against the government and especially demanding the withdrawal of Prime Minister Prayuth Chan-ocha in favor of a reformed monarchy and a more democratic constitution. In August, protesters coordinated a string of Harry Potter themed protests to publicly express their discontent in a universally recognizable manner with King Maha Vajiralongkorn representing Voldemort or “You Know Who,” the villain of the book series. This usage of “You Know Who” ironically symbolizes the lese-majeste, a series of laws making it illegal to insult, defame, or threaten the king. Raising their wands to the sky, protestors replicated the actions of Hogwarts students after the death of Dumbledore, the headmaster of the wizarding school, banding together to express a common emotion and desire. 

Because of pop culture’s widespread accessibility and relatability, it has evolved to become an integral part in enacting change. These symbols will always remain a fundamental part of our history, and hopefully, we’ll see the change they create.
