Why Do We Love True Crime?

From Jack the Ripper to Ted Bundy, the category of "true crime" has grown in popularity in the media. Netflix has countless documentaries of infamous serial killers, and research into contemporary criminal cases is simply one Google search away. But why are we so intrigued by true crime?

One reason is our innate human curiosity and interest in the unknown. Many times, people can connect with a criminal case on an emotional level and think, "That could have been me."

Additionally, true crime caters to our interest in others' lives, similar to reality television. Human beings' natural curiosity towards morbid topics may be an evolutionary mechanism, says Dr. Marissa Harrison: “Curiosity motivates us from a survival drive. When we learn about and understand lethal phenomena, we can hopefully avoid it.”

It also seems that more women are drawn to true crime, possibly stemming from another evolutionary survival tactic. Many of the victims of those who are featured in sensationalized true crime documentaries are women. Thus, by reading and learning about the case, Dr. Amanda Vicary, Ph.D., an associate professor of psychology at Illinois Wesleyan University, suggests that women may be unconsciously looking for signs of potential killers as a survival mechanism. 

Additionally, some psychologists like Dr. Meg Arroll believe true crime is a way for humans to delve into dark topics in a safe way. Psychologist Emma Kenny also suggests that true crime can connect us to our empathy, making us feel more human and also reaffirming our perception of "right and wrong.”

Overall, although there are many theories, psychologists do not truly understand why we have a fascination with true crime. One thing’s for sure though. If you’re fascinated with true crime, you’re in good company alongside the millions of people around the world who also long to satisfy this innate psychological urge.

 
 
 
 

Improving Body-Monitoring Bioelectronics

One day, we will be able to keep track of most of our physiological signals through skin-attachable technology. Currently, many wearable biosensors cause excessive redness and itching. Bioengineers at The Korea Advanced Institute of Science and Technology (KAIST) have developed a wearable silicone patch that wicks water away from skin, reducing this skin irritation.

 

Zombie Genes: An Increase of Gene Expression After Death

It has long been known that hours after we die, some cells in the body remain active. However, scientists from the University of Illinois Chicago have recently found that inflammatory glial cells enlarge for many hours after death. While this is not a major surprise, this information will play an important role in reassessing how post-mortem tissue is studied. “Our findings will be needed to interpret research on human brain tissues. We just haven't quantified these changes until now,” Dr. Jeffrey Loeb told ScienceDaily.

Leg-Breathing Trilobites

After years of debate, scientists have discovered that trilobites — extinct marine animals that lived for over 250 million years — had the upper branch of their legs function as gills. The trilobite fossils were preserved with pyrite (fool’s gold) and studied through CT scans. Because these animals are among the world’s oldest, this newfound information is valuable in understanding early animal evolution; scientists at the Hughes Lab hope to learn more about, “how life developed in response to changes in Earth's atmosphere.”

History of the Metric System

"File:Steel ruler closeup.jpg" by Ejay is licensed under CC BY-SA 4.0

Happy Monday! This week in STEM history, the metric system was established as a system of measurement for the first time in 1795. Let’s discuss how the metric system managed to dominate the international scientific community as a method of measurement. 

At the height of the French Revolution in 1795, Parisian revolutionaries had just experienced the brutal Reign of Terror that resulted in the deaths of political figures, monarchs, and everyday citizens. In an attempt to reshape Parisian and French society, a monumental change in everyday life needed to occur. This would mean that a re-establishing of every system in place would take hold. This ranged from everyday attire to the prison system to even measurement. This was because France’s Ancien Régime (the period in French history where everyone was subject to an absolute French monarch) had no formal measurement system, using around 250,000 forms of measurement in the kingdom from the 15th century to the French Revolution. Clearly, this needed to be organized. Only after the utilization of Hindu-Arabic numerals in Western European society could this occur, however. The French revolutionaries stripped the archaic methods of measurement by introducing the Republic Calendar in 1793, creating 10-hour days. This creation of 10 as a base for a standard unit of time, length, capacity, or weight (that was first proposed by Gabriel Moulton in 1670) in April 1795 by the French government established standards of measurement. This also meant that any colonies of France, including Haiti and Madras, would have converted to these methods as well. Thus, the metric system became an international system. 

Eventually, time ceased to be a part of the metric system. However, length continued to be. What would become the base unit for length? It was decided upon by Late Enlightenment thinkers in the 1790s that the meter or the length constituting one-ten millionth of the distance between the North Pole and the Equator through the Paris Meridian would become the standard unit.

In order to spread the length of the meter in Paris, mètre étalons (or standard meter bars) were placed in the city to efficiently spread the universal system (shown below). By the mid-19th century, however, the use of the meter was spreading to other Western European nations such as Britain (though the English system continued to thrive), Spain, and Switzerland. By the end of the 19th century, the units began to dominate the scientific community, which believed that an international method of sharing information would increase communication between global scientists.
