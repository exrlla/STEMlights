Mental Health during the Pandemic

The COVID-19 pandemic has drastically altered the lives and daily routines of countless people throughout the world. As a result, the mental health of many has declined, and a poll conducted by the Kaiser Family Foundation found that  “53% of adults in the United States reported that their mental health has been negatively impacted due to worry and stress over the coronavirus”.

However, the pandemic itself is not causing this drastic mental health crisis, but rather the effects of the pandemic and its toll on everyday life. High unemployment rates, a direct result of the pandemic, have been known to result in an overall increase in “depression, anxiety, distress, and low self-esteem” Furthermore, during times of stress “communication between the prefrontal cortex (the executive center of the brain) and the amygdala (the emotional processing center) may be disrupted”  leading to “the production of stress hormones that can cause distress in the body, as well as the mind.”

Now more than ever, it is vital that we look after our mental health. Scientists encourage getting adequate sleep, eating nutritious meals, and exercising, and though these things may sound arbitrary, many often neglect our most basic needs during times of high stress. Other ideas that have personally helped is creating some type of routine, learning and trying new things (such as but not limited to: baking, knitting, journaling, reading, painting, etc.!), socializing with friends via Zoom, and meditating. Additionally, overexposure to news and sensationalized media can often negatively impact your mental health and though being socially aware is important, maintaining a healthy balance is key. Mental health and self-care look different for everyone. Still, there are instances when reaching out to a medical and trained professional is necessary and getting help is never something to be ashamed of.

 
 
 
 
 
 

Abigayle Peterson

Abigayle Peterson, an incoming freshman at Western Washington University in the honors program and Distinguished Scholars of Computer Science/Math, is the Founder and CEO of Magnify Wellness— a mental health app whose mission is to ensure everyone gains equal access to wellness support.

Now free on iOS with 300+ downloads, Magnify Wellness serves as a “mental health hub” for people of all ages. Users create a personalized profile that gives them access to a gratitude journal, games, advice guides, professional counseling, a chatbot (called Maggie), among other features. #MentalHealthForever, Magnify’s open-source database, is full of additional resources for extra support.

Peterson grew passionate about mental health advocacy because of her experiences with bullying. “Although I'm not perfect either, their words hurt me a lot and dramatically affected my overall mental health,” she told STEMlights. “I realize that our words and acts can have a dramatic impact. Through coding, I desired to take my past experiences as lessons to use my voice and talents for good. ” 

Her biggest mental health tip is, “to realize that you won't always have good days, but that doesn't mean you never will. It's ok not to be productive all the time!”  Growing up, she would often play video games and journal in order to healthily cope with negative emotions, bettering her relationship with her mind. By incorporating it into the app, it can help others in the way it helped her.

As a coder and STEM student herself, Peterson understands the connection that STEM education has to mental health. “Because of STEM's rigorous, complex nature, students and parents alike feel pressure which may inhibit their ability to learn new information,” Peterson explained. “It's important to not only just raise awareness for this issue, but also take action and create more mental health support systems in our education system.” For more information on this subject, refer back to InveSTEMgations in edition 17 of STEMlights, where we explored the mental health crisis present in higher education. 

When she’s not working on Magnify Wellness, Peterson does machine learning research, writes for Reinvented Magazine, works as a leader at CodeHers Collective (teaching girls how to code), and has won many awards for her ventures. She’s also passionate about racial equity and AI for social good. 

“Magnify is my drive and gives me empowerment from within to face my struggles with anxiety instead of running away; it fills me with a passion that I’m potentially able to make someone else’s life better with my skills in computer science.”

To learn more about Peterson and Magnify Wellness, check out Magnify’s website or Instagram. 

 
 

Social distancing is scientifically proven.                                                                              A new study by Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health found that social distancing during COVID-19 significantly decreases the likelihood of contracting the virus. Survey participants who used public transportation or visited high traffic locations were more likely to have contracted coronavirus at one point. Researchers analyzed the different variables that could have affected the data but found that after adjusting for movement and social distancing, infection history is primarily controlled by those two factors. 

Possibility of life on Venus                                                                                        Scientists discovered phosphine, a molecule that could indicate life, in one of the clouds in Venus. Although it was found in very small amounts, phosphine is the byproduct of microbes living in environments without oxygen. The phosphine could also be an off-shoot of non-biological processes, but the researchers believe it is unlikely as it would only account for a small fraction of the amount of phosphine found. With more research, the possibility of life on Venus could be a breakthrough discovery for the astrobiology field. 

3D Printed Milk Products                                                                                                While 3D printed food products have been around for some time, milk-based food products are a little more difficult to 3D print due to its temperature-sensitive nature, which typically requires heat and high-temperature extrusion methods. However, using a cold-extrusion method and powdered milk to print products, dairy products can now be printed. This new method could pave the way for developing special food products.

SuicideStop Mental Health Help Center

Especially with the pandemic hindering in-person contact, it can be hard to interact with others, nonetheless, ask for help. SuicideStop is a way for you to be able to chat with a professionally trained listener without the need to be face to face or verbally talking. Your information will remain confidential, anything you reveal will not be shared, and it’s easily accessible to use. The center is free of charge, so you don’t have to worry about any fees. If you’ve been meaning to reach out for help but haven’t yet, perhaps SuicideStop will be a good start.
