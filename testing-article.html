Finland’s Education System

Most people believe that education is the gateway to success; it gives people the tools to understand the world around them. Therefore, there is no doubt that the quality of one’s education is vital. Good quality education entails teaching students how to learn, encouraging them to form their own opinions, and helping them develop critical thinking skills. One country that continues to make headlines regarding the quality of education it offers is Finland. Finland is widely known to have one of the leading education systems in the world. 

Children in Finland are not required to start school until the age of seven. Once enrolled in school, books, excursions, and school lunches are free for all students. Students in Finland are also given less homework than students in their age range in other countries. Throughout their entire primary and secondary school years, Finland students are not required to take standardized tests until around 16. Most students opt to take the exams out of curiosity, and their scores are not publicized. Kari Louhivuori, a school’s principal in Finland, stated that,“It’s [standardized test results] nonsense. We know much more about the children than these tests can tell us.” Finland’s idea of not prioritizing standardized tests presents a sharp contrast to schools in the United States, which are mainly defined by testing and data.

One of the most remarkable things about Finland’s school system is that each school receives the same amount of funds. In other words, there is no such thing as “private school.” This policy prevents a child’s socioeconomic status from being an obstacle in his or her academic success. This strategy in Finland differs from the U.S. where zip code, race, and parent’s income, more often than not, determines a child’s quality of education.

Aside from these educational policies, teachers in Finland have a great deal of influence on student learning. About ten percent of the nation’s college graduates are selected to be teachers. To become teachers, they are required to earn a master’s degree in education. Schools and teachers decide how the curriculum is taught in their classrooms. Finland respects the teachers; it is also not unusual for teachers to earn a salary close to that of a doctor. While many parents in the U.S. spend hours considering how their children can get better grades and get into better schools, in Finland, parents hardly worry about finding the right school for their children.

Dr. Brooke Moyers

As we hit the 50th edition of the STEMlights newsletter, our subscriber count is 690+ strong! What else has been growing at a rapid rate? The global human population in is now at 7.8 billion people, and it’s growing faster by the second. Concerns for the future of food security and sustainable farming in our ecosystems continue to grow. Dr. Brooke Moyers is working towards a solution.

How did she get here? Moyers grew up in California, and with support from her parents, obtained a high school scholarship and became the first in her family to attend college. She continued to dedicate her life to research in biology, earning her PhD. Moyers currently works as a professor at the University of Massachusetts Boston. Moyers was always, “filled with joy when plants start to grow and flower again,” in the spring. Her love of plants blended with her desire to combat food insecurity, one of the world’s most severe issues. 

Her work circles around understanding the interactions between genetics and the environment, and how that can influence the ways that plants can grow. In addition to mentoring biologists-in-traning, Dr. Moyers teaches classes on population biology, evolutionary genomics. Her main goal is to research ways to grow rice—a staple food for many people—with less water, for the sake of future food security. Check out her research here!

A common misconception in her field is the idea that scientists are always in the lab wearing goggles and white coats. Dr. Moyers describes her daily life to be a mix of teaching, analyzing data, and occasionally going out into the fields to do experiments! Another common misconception is the idea that life is only shaped either by nature or nurtured habits. Do your traits reflect your genetic inheritance or your environment? Both actually have an impact!

Looking back on her journey, Dr. Moyers tells STEMlights that in a career such as hers, the most important classes to take are related to, "data analysis/computer science/statistics classes, and classes in writing, especially persuasive or nonfiction writing." She advises prospective researchers to spread their nets wide, seek out diverse research experiences, and talk to professors. You never know what you’ll find!

Blast to the Past: 50 Years of Extraordinary Science

During the year 1971, 50 years ago from the year 2021, many advancements within the field of science mark their 50th jubilee. These discoveries can be attributed to fields like medicine, computer science, biology, astronomy, and even environmental science.

Apollo 14 Moon Landing

On February 9th, 1971, Apollo 14 made a successful landing on the Moon, characterized as the third human Moon landing in history. During this mission, the astronauts detected moonquakes (ultimately helping with the characterization of the Moon’s interior) and identified solar wind/atmospheric composition in addition to the exploration of the Moon’s plasma environment.

  Discovery of Place Cells

In the year of 1971, there were also several advancements within the field of biology. John O’Keefe, an American-British neuroscientist, became well-known for his discovery of the place cell located in the hippocampus region of the brain (frequently associated with memory). These place cells are commonly characterized as cognitive images of spatial locations, mimicking a cognitive map.

Artificial Intelligence

The world is increasingly becoming automated. Industries and economies all over are embracing the prospects of a future where computers make our lives easier. Revenues from the Artificial Intelligence (AI) market are predicted to grow 16.4% in 2021 to $327.5 billion, according to the Worldwide Semiannual Artificial Intelligence Tracker. However, amidst all this growth potential, there has always been a deep-seated fear that has prevailed, a question that has no answer and that many have dismissed as paranoia: Will AI take over the human race? Or, how much power should we give to AI?

The late Marvin Minsky, the renowned American cognitive and computer scientist, commented that, "The next generation of computers will be so intelligent that we will be lucky if they are willing to keep us around the house as household pets," a testament to the power that humans may give to AI in the future. Granted that this question may not be feasible in the near future, it has garnered attention from big names like Elon Musk, Lex Fridman, Google, and Amazon, and it is worthwhile to explore its consequences.

One goal for the advancement of AI has been to build machines that can pass the Turing test. However, it is important to think past the automotive aspect and think about the ethics of AI advancement. In 1942, renowned science fiction writer and biochemist Isaac Asimov stated his three laws of robotics that have been the subject of criticism over the decades. They are: 

1. A robot may not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm.

2. A robot must obey orders given to it by human beings except where such orders would conflict with the First Law.

3. A robot must protect its own existence as long as such protection does not conflict with the First or Second Law.

Granted that the definition of words and phrases like “injure" and “inaction” can be put to the metal as being subjective, these three laws hold something in common: ethics. As long as humans continue to build AI, there is a need to set aside monetary-based interests and work towards the greater good: the betterment of humanity.

The goal is to manufacture AI machines that are “friendly," that is, whose goals are human-centric. Moral reasoning and autonomy are subjects that need to be analyzed through a much more detailed lens for them to be applied to the next generation of AI.
