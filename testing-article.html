It's Time to Act

Climate change is the change in the average weather patterns of a place. The causes of climate change are largely attributed to human activity and a growing world population. For the past 50 years, climate change has made headlines as an issue of great concern. According to a research study by NASA, 2020 tied with 2016 for the warmest year on record, with both years documenting many extreme weather events, including hurricanes, wildfires, and droughts. 

Every day, scientists are making discoveries regarding how global warming is affecting the planet we live on. It can be disheartening to hear that our planet is at risk, but it is important to note that saving ourselves from the far-reaching consequences of climate change requires a collective effort. On April 22, a day recognized as Earth Day, President Biden held a Global Leaders Summit on Climate Change. At the meeting, several companies, governmental organizations, and financial institutions stated their commitment to net-zero emissions. Net-zero refers to achieving a balance between the number of greenhouse gases released and the number of greenhouse gases removed from the atmosphere. 

During the summit, Biden planned to reach a net-zero emission goal and reduce the United States’ dependence on fossil fuels by 2050. This decision marks a turning point in the discussion about climate change, as the U.S. ranks second in regards to greenhouse gas emission contributions. In spite of critiques, there is evidence that shows that climate action is necessary to create sustainable jobs, promote cleaner air, and promote a sustainable food supply.

Although global warming is primarily caused by activity in developed nations, the developing world is disproportionately affected by it. Developing countries are more likely to experience the effects of global warming first and they are also likely to be the hardest hit. One developing country facing the impacts of climate change is China. China is particularly unique because although it is still in its developing stage, it ranks first in greenhouse gas emission contributions. The country is responsible for more than 25% of the earth’s annual carbon emissions. 

One tangible effect of climate change affecting the country is air pollution, with wearing air-filtering face masks becoming the norm for people living in China. Studies suggest that if global warming continues on the path it is on right now, China will experience more frequent periods of smog and heat waves, causing debilitating health problems for the country’s citizens. This problem is also concerning because it will expose future generations to lifelong health issues.

Climate change is indeed a complex issue to address, and although it is difficult to prepare for every possibility, one thing is certain: climate change is the crisis of our time, and the time to act is now.

Catilyn Widjaja: Executive Director of Girl Steminist

Tell us about your journey.

Growing up, I never envisioned myself wanting to go into STEM because I didn’t want to be seen as “nerdy” by other kids. As I grew up, I realized there was nothing bad about being seen this way and I grew to explore my love of technology and biology within classes at my school, coding camps, and hackathons.

I had always felt discouraged because when I envisioned STEM I pictured men with no interests other than lab experiments and staring at computer screens all day — but after realizing my true interests in STEM, I’m now super passionate about empowering other young women to pursue STEM to get more representation in the fields so in the future younger girls don’t have to deny themselves from being immersed in it.

What is the main goal of Girl STEMinist?

Girl STEMinist is a student-led organization dedicated to bridging the gender gap in STEM by empowering young women to pursue careers in STEM through career exploration, collaboration, and community. The main way we do this is through the publication of our Digital Journals and Instagram platform. Our Digital Journals aim to inspire and empower young women through showcasing different careers in STEM according to our industry of focus, showcase professionals, articles on career and personal development, artwork, etc.

We aim to collaborate and create a positive community while working on something to help empower young women in STEM and bring inspiration to join the fields! Our applications for our Second Issue on Healthcare are currently open right now. If any readers would like to check out the roles and responsibilities and apply check out the link here!

What have you learned through this process and what would you like to tell others trying to start the same? 

I think something that helped me was identifying why I am starting a student-led organization and transforming my passion for advocating for more women in STEM into an actionable way through building a supportive community via Digital Journal teams. But, starting out was definitely difficult. I struggled doing things on my own and realized that there are so many amazing young women out there with the same vision who I could rely on. I would advise others trying to do the same to make sure to have a clear mission and distinct purpose to why you’re doing what you’re doing. Once you identify a clear and unique purpose, you’ll start to attract individuals who have the same passion to create impact together. With the support of my team members and Directors, the process has definitely been a lot more manageable and fun!

Visit girlsteminist.org and check out their Instagram, @girlsteminist, for more information!

 
 

Parkinson’s Disease Awareness

The overall cause of Parkinson’s Disease (PD) remains unknown to this day. What researchers do know is that 10-15% of Parkinson’s cases are influenced by genetics with the remaining cases titled as “sporadic”. Additionally, treatments of PD can vary from person to person. Methods of treatment range from medication (most frequent treatment) to surgical remedies and lifestyle adjustments. As of recently, more than 10 million people worldwide are diagnosed with PD, with more than 60,000 Americans diagnosed annually.

Ways to Combat Stress

In light of Stress Awareness Month, it is important to recognize methods to healthily manage stress. One way to reduce stress is through physical activity like taking a walk through a park. Doing so will decrease the production of stress hormones like adrenaline and cortisol while increasing the pump of endorphins into the brain, commonly known to stimulate natural pain relief. In addition to getting your body moving, a healthy diet can lower blood pressure and decrease the production of the aforementioned stress-inducing hormones, reducing stress levels.

Autism Spectrum Disorder Awareness

1 in 54 children is diagnosed with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) with the majority of cases linked to genetics. Children of older parents are at a much greater risk of being diagnosed with autism than children of younger parents, with up to a 10% greater chance. Speech, occupational therapy, and Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) are all common remedial practices associated with the enhancement of developmental skills when managing ASD.

 

To Infinity And Beyond: Signs Of Water On The Moon May Heat Up Lunar Exploration

The moon has been an object of great fascination for humankind. Today, one of the biggest aims of space exploration is the detection of water, and its various forms, on other celestial bodies. With such a discovery, there would be hope for human relocation.

 Recently, a team of scientists led by Paul Hayne, an assistant professor in the laboratory of Atmospheric and Space Physics at the University of Colorado Boulder, obtained proof that there might be more lunar water than was previously assumed. This team’s mission was to deduce the surface area occupied by Permanent Shadowed Regions (PSRs). PSRs are dark areas on the moon that do not receive sunlight. The scientists wanted to verify what portion of these PSRs have a surface temperature of 110 K or less and evaluate the latitude at which water-ice is likely to form.

Hayne and his team examined PSRs on the polar regions of the moon since water would be more protected there from the sun’s rays. They found that beyond 80 degrees latitude, subzero temperatures, suitable for the existence of water-ice, become more prevalent. Upon investigation, they found that within the PSRs, there exist areas known as ‘cold traps’, where the frigid temperatures allow volatiles, such as water, to remain stable.

The team set out to conclusively prove not just the existence of cold traps and micro-cold traps on the moon, but also their extent. The cumulative area of the cold traps and micro-cold traps is the same size as Switzerland, with the most numerous cold traps only 1 cm in magnitude.

Although water-ice had previously been presumed to exist on the lunar surface, Hayne’s study of cold traps and PSRs has profound implications. For one, cold traps guarantee that trapped water may stay present for a long time. In Hayne’s words: “[I]f water gets in there, it is not going anywhere for a billion years.” By confirming the existence of more cold traps, Hayne and his team have helped set in motion what could be a new era of lunar exploration, and even settlement – one that could commence with the NASA Artemis program. The invaluable water-ice could even be used to fuel rockets and rovers, offsetting the expenses of extraterrestrial exploration. 

Soon, as we gaze to the night sky, we may yet see lunar encampments, a giant leap forward in the advancement of human space exploration.

 

NASA Solve

Ever dreamed of helping NASA solve some of the their toughest problems? NASA Solve invites individual members of the public to, "advance research, solve problems, and potentially win prizes as a result of their work." Opportunities — open to anyone in the general public — range from their annual CineSpace Short Film Challenge to their Break the Ice Lunar Challenge, which asks for ideas on how to, "excavate the Moon’s icy regolith, or dirt, and deliver it to a hypothetical processing plant at the lunar South Pole." Prizes include cash awards and internships. Previous challenges have allowed students to design and build experiments for International Space Station! Who knows, maybe your one brilliant idea can help NASA with their next interstellar adventure!
