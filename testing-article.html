The Green New Deal: Misconceptions 

The Green New Deal (GND) is one of the most polarizing pieces of legislation whose main purpose is to address the climate crisis. You can think of it as a broad to-do list for the United States that would take place gradually over the next ten years. Such goals include: “achieving net-zero greenhouse gas emissions, establishing millions of high-wage jobs and ensuring economic security for all, investing in infrastructure and industry, securing a sustainable environment for all, and promoting justice and equality.” However, the GND has been highly controversial due to the many misconceptions that surround it.

One big misconception is that it will bankrupt the United States. Though the GND is estimated to cost 93 trillion dollars over the next ten years, it will not bankrupt the country. If anything, the damages that the climate crisis will inevitably cause, if left unchecked, will far surpass the money spent to prevent it.

Another misconception is that it will ‘take away our meat.’ The truth is factory farming is responsible for nearly half of the United States greenhouse emissions and any comprehensive conversation about climate change must address the issues within the animal agriculture industry as well. “In fact, scientists agree that a global reduction in meat intake could potentially cut global greenhouse emissions by 50%." Though the GND does not plan to eliminate all meat, it does want to reduce the overall meat in the average American’s diet, thus making it slightly more expensive.

Lastly, it will take away cars and planes. The GND plans to “invest in a carbon-neutral transportation system that improves all of our lives. It looks like saving a lot of time and money with innovations like a new electrified high-speed rail that will make short trips between hubs such as Boston and New York easier.” Cars and planes will not be eliminated but rather, the GND hopes to decrease the demand for them by improving and investing in more eco-friendly alternatives.

Though climate change has become a highly politicized topic, the GND simply plans to invest in preventative measures against the climate crisis to overall better the standard of living for many Americans both now and in the future.

 
 
 
 
 
 

Mahir Rahman

With a goal of equalizing educational opportunities, STEMlights’ Program Lead and STEMchats Internal Development Lead Mahir Rahmin recognizes the importance of political engagement. Rahman is an IB senior at Gateway High School in Kissimmee, Florida looking to study Public Health and Political Science while on a pre-law track after high school. 

In transitioning from a Program Coordinator leading the Politics x STEM section to Program Lead and Internal Development  Lead, Rahman has grown passionate about bringing equitable educational opportunities to students. “I am the STEMlights Program Lead and the Internal Development Advisor. This mainly consists of working, organizing, and structuring the STEMlights Team and the weekly newsletters that are sent out to our lovely readers!” Rahman said. “I became the lead in April, and in just 6 months, STEMlights has seen rebranding, an increase of 500 subscribers, a team double its original, and 26 newsletters! As the Internal Development Advisor, my role is to work with the Internal Development Team to foster healthy team relationships and team culture. More importantly, through my work with STEMlights and Internal Development.” 

Rahman found out about STEMchats after getting tagged on a STEMchats Instagram post then decided to apply. Upon his acceptance into the STEMlights program, Rahman followed up a commitment to political engagement by beginning the Politics x STEM section.   

“I wanted to bridge the gap between politics, STEM, and the real-world while providing information that will legitimately provoke readers to think in an open-minded manner. Realistically, I also am not a hardcore-STEM fan. I truly enjoy some aspects of STEM, who doesn't? But more importantly, I enjoy the intersections of STEM, including social sciences and politics. More importantly, I saw that people around me just didn’t know what was going on (politically),” Rahman explained. “It struck me—I had the opportunity to write, so I should curate articles that would allow people to not only obtain a STEM education but one that would foster an interest in politics. I wrote about so many fascinating topics, and it truly showed me that STEMlights could be more than a typical STEM newsletter, but one that employed the intersections of STEM.”

Both within and beyond STEMlights, Rahman maintains the fundamental importance of political engagement in pursuing an equitable quality of life.

“Politics is the center of the world. Whether you like it or not, everything we do is political,” Rahman noted.  “I recognize that politics has shaped my status—from the educational resources I get to the lack of communication within my community—politics is at the center of it all. It can easily take over health systems, technological access, and even educational material. Being in Florida and the most underfunded county within the State, I have been more engaged in Progressive Politics, as it allows for an equal shot at life. I can continue rambling about why politics is so crucial, vital, or even essential to our daily lives, but the one thing I have to say is: whether you like it or not, everything you do, every step you take, and every word you type, is political. It is important to take part in a political system, especially as an FGLI, to represent the voice that will always be taken away from you in today’s society.” 

Rahman has done extensive political work beyond STEMlights. “I do not enjoy STEM, ironically, so a lot of my work is social-justice and social-science focused,” Rahman noted. “A lot of my work is with the Jane Goodall Institute and the Roots and Shoots National Youth Leadership Council. Aside from the Honor societies at school, I also pour a lot of my energy into my Roots and Shoots club at school, where we are planning to do a Women’s Wellness Project, a Speaker Series, and more! You can find our work at rootsandshoots.org! I have also worked on political campaigns, which furthered my interest in the field.”

Looking forward, Rahman has a bright future in mind for STEMlights and academic aspirations. “Aside from STEMchats, I would actually like to start my own non-profit (in the future) that would aim to promote equal legal and medical opportunities for immigrant minority families. My nearest goal right now is to get into a good school that offers a strong Public Health program, in hopes of getting my MPH/JD in the future! With STEMlights, I want to keep providing Weekly newsletters, get our podcast, and other fun programs we have rolling as soon as possible!”

 
 

Key-words trigger conservative and liberal brains differently.                                According to a study by the University of California, Berkeley, neural activity differs in the brains of conservatives and liberals, particularly to vocabulary in the topics of threat, morality, and emotions. This phenomenon is called neural polarization and involves a part of the brain called the dorsomedial prefrontal cortex, which is in charge of narratives. Participants with brain activity, similar to a political group’s average brain activity were most likely to identify with the corresponding political view. 

Average body temperature decreasing.                                                                                For the past decade, doctors cite 98.6°F as the average temperature for the human body. However, a new study by the University of California, Santa Barbara found that over recent years, the average human temperature to be lower by around 1°F in adults in both the UK and the U.S. and in forager horticulturists called the Tsimane. This is most likely the result of modern medicine and a drop in infections. 

Water was discovered on the moon.                                                                                      NASA discovered water on the Clavius Crater, which is on the southern hemisphere of the Moon and located on a sunlit surface. Previously, scientists believed water was only located on the cold, shadowed surfaces on the Moon, but the discovery proves water could be distributed everywhere across the Moon.

Voter Toolkits

Elections and voting can be confusing, especially if it’s your first time. To help ease your anxious and overwhelming thoughts, the American Library Association has created Voting & Election Toolkits! There’s one for each state in the USA, so you can click on yours respectively for more specific resources. Pre-Registering to Vote

For the ones who can’t vote yet, you can still prepare yourself for when the time comes. The age varies from state to state, but for most, as long as you’re 16 years old, you can pre-register to vote! Pre-registering means that you will be in the voting registration system even though you can’t legally vote yet, and your status will automatically activate once you turn 18. Doing so will eliminate the worry of registering later on when you are of age, and you’ll most likely be more inclined to vote when you’re actually able to if you’re already in the system.
