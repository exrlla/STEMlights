Bee Venom Therapy or Torture? 

Within the past couple of decades, many holistic medicinal practices have increased in popularity throughout the western world. One, in particular, that has garnered a lot of attention and simultaneous scrutiny is bee venom therapy; a practice in which the individual will purposefully sting themselves in various pressure points throughout their body. “In China, Korea, Indonesia, and parts of South America, bee acupuncture has long been part of traditional medicine. In fact, it has been used to treat the pain of arthritis and other rheumatic diseases.” 

However this therapy does not come without risks and many scientists wonder if there is any validity behind this practice at all: “While bee venom does contain an anti-inflammatory compound known as melittin, medical studies have not provided any convincing evidence that this component is efficacious in alleviating pain or in treating the purported medical conditions.” There have been some studies that show melittin can kill bacteria in a petri dish but no proven conclusions that it will work in humans. Furthermore,  “in a 2015 study published in PLOS One noted that “compared with normal saline injection, bee venom acupuncture showed a 261% increased relative risk for the occurrence of adverse events”. 

At this point in time, the risks associated with this controversial treatment outweigh the possible positives and though there have been positive reviews online from people with personal experiences, consulting a doctor or medical professional is best.

 
 
 
 
 
 

Ava Monroe

A senior drama major at Laguardia High School, 17-year old Ava Monroe is the executive director of the Cure Campaign — a nationwide campaign to pass federal legislation to increase the National Cancer Research budget and prevent proposed NIH [National Institutes of Health] cuts. Run by a team of 15 teenagers, they hope, “to not only change America, but to make a difference in the lives of the 590,000 people and their families affected by cancer every year.”

Ava started the campaign when she was 15 years old, five years after her mother was first diagnosed with breast cancer. “There is an almost palatable feeling of hopelessness when someone you love is diagnosed; you can’t help but feel like there is nothing you can do. And, there’s always the fear of recurrence. I was overwhelmed by this fear for my mom, and I knew I couldn’t just sit back and let it control me. So, after getting involved with a couple of other movements -- seeing what I liked and didn’t like in terms of leadership -- I started The Cure. Now, here we are, two years later,” Ava told STEMlights.

The Cure has run a number of initiatives to advance its mission. Ava’s personal favorite, the 50in50 Campaign, encourages youth (one from each state) to make a local impact-- working on community service projects like fundraising or volunteer work, all while honing in on cancer research funding. Along the road to congressional approval, she hopes to inspire youth advocacy nationwide.

 “...there is an effortless power in youthful naivety… we owe it to ourselves to stay informed and educated, and to listen as much as we speak. When we do so, we become catalysts for societal change and equity, because we establish ourselves as a new generation secure in our ideals,” Ava explained. “I am so proud to be a part of Gen Z, and I’ve seen some incredible activists and artists alike make some huge strides in creating a better world for us all. I believe in youth advocacy because I believe in hope, and we are this planet’s only hope.”

Aside from her work at The Cure, Ava is a multi-talented performing artist. Right now, she is continuing work on her album, auditioning and solidifying projects, and finalizing her college applications. She is also working on The Cure’s next initiative, a labor-intensive research project in solidarity with the Black Lives Matter movement.

“I may not be a doctor, and I may not understand the scientific side of funding cancer research, but I do know that everyone knows someone who has/had/or beat cancer. I hope that, together, we can turn our pain into power by educating ourselves about the radical lack of federal funding for cancer research and doing something about it.”

 Learn more about Ava by following her @avamonr0e. For more information about The Cure, check out their Instagram or website, https://thecure.world/.

 
 

Coastal flooding affects 31 million people                                                               Scientists from Indiana University analyzed river deltas in geographic regions around the world to estimate the number of people that could potentially be affected by floods. River deltas are increasingly more prone to storm surges and floods as climate change increases ocean level rise. Surprisingly, river deltas make up only 0.5% of the land on Earth but contain 4.5% of the world’s population. This is the first study to analyze river deltas.

More efficient plastic-eating enzyme process                                                              PETase, a plastic-eating bacteria, is now being combined with another enzyme, MHETase, to speed up the plastic breakdown process. PETase breaks down polyethylene terephthalate (PET), which greatly reduces the greenhouse gas and plastic pollution associated with climate change. With MHETase, the process is now six times faster and spells hope for the future of climate change.

Dark matter ingredient unknown                                                                                 NASA’s Hubble telescope and European Southern Observatory's Very Large Telescope have found that there is a mysterious unknown ingredient in dark matter that scientists have yet to uncover. This ingredient could account for the inconsistency found between theoretical computer simulations and dark matter observations in galaxy clusters. By figuring out this ingredient, scientists could potentially fully understand the mechanism of dark matter.
