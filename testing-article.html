The Rise of Genetic Engineering

Genetic engineering is the process of editing an organism's genetic composition through recombinant DNA (rDNA) technology. The first genetically modified organism was created almost five decades ago by scientists Herbert Boyer and Stanley Cohen. However, a study shows that genetic engineering has existed for over 30,000 years though not in the highly sophisticated way we are witnessing today. We, humans, have implemented genetic engineering on various organisms because of the benefits it provides. The genetic modification of plants and animals has led to more nutritious, delicious food, increased food supply, medicinal foods, and an increase in favorable traits among a species. Although there are many benefits to genetic engineering, there are many drawbacks that have yet to be fully understood. 

Over the last decade, the topic of human genetic modification has made the headlines. Both scientists and the general public alike have mixed feelings about genetic modification. The recurring question is, should we, as a society, approve the genetic engineering of future humans? What does this mean for the future of humanity in terms of ethics and morality? Other questions to consider include: how can we mitigate the social inequalities if we choose to go on with genetic enhancement? How will genetic engineering change the evolutionary process?

Genetic engineering holds the promise of allowing humans to have better memory, stronger muscles and giving them the ability to choose the sex of their children or even reverse aging. Genetically enhanced humans are often referred to as “superhumans.” One side of the argument against genetic engineering believes that it is an unethical means of improving oneself. Many argue that genetic engineering should not be pursued because of its biological, moral, ethical, socioeconomic, political implications. The other side of the argument believes that genetic engineering has the potential to offer remedies to genetic diseases that plague the human race.

As we continue to analyze the duality of natural selection and genetic engineering, we must take into account that genetic engineering will impose a great amount of responsibility on us, humans. The extent to which we genetically modify ourselves will determine the level of accountability we must hold ourselves to.

Leaves and Color Changes

Due to changes in temperature and the length of daylight during the Fall, the food-making process, also known as photosynthesis, used in leaves during the spring and summer, slows down. Chlorophyll, the pigment associated with a leaf's green color, begins to break down and no longer supplies leaves with their green pigmentation. In addition to leaves containing the chlorophyll pigment, they also contain carotenes and xanthophyll pigments that enhance their orange and yellow colors. Once the green pigment begins to lose its intensity, the orange and yellow colors become more visible just like how we witness during Autumn's arrival.

 

Dragon Blood Tree

Dragon Blood trees (Dracaena cinnabari), or the Socotra dragon tree, is a tree native to Yemen’s Socotra Archipelago, near the Arabian sea. This evergreen tree grows up to 12 meters (39 feet) high and can live as long as 650 years. Its shape resembles an upside-down tree crown, much like an umbrella canopy flipping inside out during a windy storm. The red fleshy fruit of the tree that is the main source of its nickname, takes about five months to grow to maturity. The resin produced by this fruit is used as a varnish for violins and even has medicinal qualities associated with it.

Nutrition and Mental Health

A recently published study showed that from the sampled students in a UK secondary school, those who had an intake of five or more fruit and vegetable portions also reported better mental health results. The research team associated with this study, UEA Health and Social Care Partners, noted that better quality in nutrition helps with children’s mental health which ultimately allows them to reach their full potential. One interesting finding was that the quality of nutrition had a much greater impact on mental health than other factors, like witnessing potential arguments or violence at home. The overall goal of the research team was to illustrate the connection between nutrition and mental health and push for greater policy implementations within school districts.

Ellen Ochoa

nasa.gov

In celebration of Hispanic Heritage Month, let’s spotlight one of the most accomplished NASA veterans to date. 

Ellen Ochoa, born in 1958, grew up in California and pursued a life of academics. She earned her bachelor’s degree in physics at San Diego State University and then her master’s and doctorate in electrical engineering at Stanford. At the age of 32, she was asked to go up into space to conduct research on the Earth’s ozone layer. She would go up into orbit for a total of 4 trips and over 950 hours of spaceflight. Her work didn’t stop there either. She would later become the director of the Johnson Space Center.  

In an interview with Harvard Kennedy School, Ochoa discusses her thirty years at NASA. She tells us that even when she began working there as a research engineer, she realized that NASA had a commitment to diversity and equality between genders and race, something that she seldom saw. However, the leadership positions had still been predominantly white and male. Gradually, she and other women would begin to fill the ranks. 

Ochoa talks about her trips up in microgravity, and she tells us about the thin blue line around Earth. She tells us that her view of the world gave her the understanding that our world is extraordinary in its fragility. It’s one thin line between life as we know it and the vacuum of space. Yet, that fragility gave her hope. Being able to see the atmospheric horizon, she comes to the realization that anything is possible. As long as there are enough brilliant minds and resources put to a task, there is nothing that humanity cannot accomplish.

She acts as a shining beacon, as a pioneering female and Hispanic astronaut, for those in the future. We as readers can take her message and let her be an inspiration to ourselves as we reach towards the final frontier.

Cambridge Future Scholar Research Program

The Cambridge Center for International Research (CCIR) is hosting an academic research program aimed towards high school students (preferably juniors - seniors level)! It’s open internationally and selects to provide small, personalized classes with educators and staff from Oxbridge. There are 21 research courses under the program, ranging under four categories of STEM, business, social sciences, and humanities. Each course includes lectures and a capstone research project that may even be published as part of a journal publication! It should be noted that this opportunity costs tuition, but almost 50% of last year’s cohort were provided scholarships to participate. Early admissions deadline has passed, but regular decision closes October 23rd.

STEM on the Sidelines Challenge Minecraft / Robotics

University of California, Irvine’s (UCI) engineering school is partnering with three other major programs to create two design challenges for high school teams. The first challenge is focused on engineering a prototype that can be controlled to “kick” a mini football towards a target. This challenge is in person for those living in the southern California region, and UCI will be providing workshops and some materials for participants. The second challenge involves building a Minecraft world ( :p ). This one is completely online, and submissions will be sent through a video format. Teams must build a Los Angeles Chargers football themed world that integrates components of STEM throughout. UCI may provide some teams raspberry pi’s for those who can’t access Minecraft platforms. There will be multiple winners and prizes include UCI gear, gift cards, Chargers swag, and tickets to one of their games! The deadline to register was extended to this Friday, October 22nd, so grab a team and sign up soon. For more information, contact: mesa@uci.edu.
