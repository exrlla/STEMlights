Stop AAPI Hate

Identifying and addressing anti-Asian discrimination (and hate against any group of people) is essential to dismantling it. Even before the murder of eight victims in Atlanta on March 16, six of them Asian-American women, racism against the AAPI (Asian-Americans and Pacific Islanders) community has always been present. President Biden addressed the issue by stating that “vicious hate crimes against Asian-Americans who have been attacked, harassed, blamed, and scapegoated are wrong, un-American, and must stop.” It is crucial to recognize that anti-Asian sentiment in the United States has existed for hundreds of years. Los Angeles, for one, has a long history of Asian discrimination. On October 24, 1871, a mob of whites and Hispanics robbed, attacked, and murdered 19 Chinese residents, including a 15-year-old boy, in Old Chinatown. 

In 1875, the Page Exclusion Act was enacted, prohibiting Asian women from immigrating to the United States. At one point, they were called “sexually immoral prostitutes.” In 1882, the Chinese Exclusion Act was passed, prohibiting the immigration of Chinese laborers; it was not until 1943 that the act was annulled. During the Second World War, around 120,000 Japanese people were forced into internment camps. 

On June 19, 1982, a Chinese American man named Vincent Chin was beaten to death at a party by two white Detroit auto workers who assumed he was Japanese. A dancer at the party recounts one of the men shouting at Chin, “it is because of you [expletive] that we’re out of work.” During that time, many white auto workers believed that Japanese auto workers were to blame for the decline of automotive manufacturing jobs. At the trial, the men were each fined $3,000 and went scot-free. This “punishment” sparked a national outcry and propelled the Asian-American rights movement.

The amount of racist remarks and attacks has heightened since the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic. There have been almost 3,800 Anti-Asian hate incidents, reported between March 19, 2020, and February 28, 2021. Unfortunately, this number may be inaccurate because some Asian-Americans are afraid to report hate crimes due to language barriers or immigration status. Asian hate and racism escalated this past year due to racist rhetoric and negative comments made by several leaders, including former president Donald Trump. During his time in office, he repeatedly referred to the coronavirus as the “China virus," the “Wuhan virus,” and the “Kung flu” on numerous platforms. 

This issue should teach us not to stay silent or be bystanders when we witness racism, no matter who is in power. It is evident that the issue of anti-Asian hate has become rampant, and we must put a stop to hate crimes and violence perpetrated against the community. Posting solidarity statements on social media is simply not enough to show our support—we must check up on our friends in the AAPI community, educate ourselves about the long history of anti-Asian racism, and strive towards becoming anti-racist.

Lady Mary Wortley Montagu

As the COVID-19 pandemic slowly edges towards recovery, over 14% of the American population has been fully vaccinated. Humanity has slowly been able to respond to epidemics and health crises faster and faster throughout the century, and much of it can be attributed back to the lady in the black-and-white picture: Lady Mary Wortley Montagu. 

Born in 1689, Lady Montagu was a prolific writer. Her legacy entail a series of letters and works as a novelist and eccentric poet. She travelled around Europe during her life time, writing letters to her family all the while, detailing a life of simplicity until her death in 1762. Despite an attribution to the arts, one of Lady Montagu’s most important works to the modern day regards to her travels to the Ottoman Empire.

In the 52 Turkish embassy essays, she details her life in the Ottoman Empire, where the concept of inoculation against the small pox disease was more common. Before the invention of the small pox vaccine in 1796, the disease accounted for up to 1 in every 13 deaths in the world. Innoculations, defined as a method of artificially inducing immunity against a disease, were being practiced by the Chinese since the 10th century but had not yet made a break through in the Western Hemisphere. 

Lady Montagu herself had facial scars from small pox as a young woman and fought for the protection of children against the disease as well. She had the chance to see the Turkish inoculation methods with her husband, Henry Montagu, who was the Turkish Embassador for Britain at the time. Impressed by their work, Montagu promptly had her own children inoculated against the small pox vaccine and began to draw attention to inoculation amongst the upper-tier of European society.

The practice continued to proliferate until it became commonplace. Lady Montagu pioneered the path to a safer and healthier world. In the 21st century, three centuries after her death, the Moderna, Pfizer, Johnson-Johnson vaccines can trace some origins back to Montagu. 

 

Seaweed, Cattle, and Greenhouse Gas Emissions

A serving of seaweed does not only contain a dose of vitamins and minerals sufficient for our health, but it also has significant benefits in decreasing greenhouse gases, at least those produced by cattle. If just a small amount of seaweed gets added to cattle feed, the methane emissions produced by cattle through respiratory activity can drop by as much as 82% as suggested by recent discoveries.

March: National Kidney Awareness Month

Normally, kidneys are about the size of a cell phone with a weight of approximately 5.5 ounces. Although the weight of a kidney is a small percentage of a human’s body weight, a quarter of the body’s blood supply passes through the kidneys, filtered through the millions of nephrons that add up to a distance of approximately 10 miles if positioned linearly side-to-side.

  Spring Welcoming and the Case of Dandelions:

As spring makes its way every year, most places observe the chirping songs of birds or the tranquil pouring of spring showers. However, an additional observation involves the sprouting of bright yellow dandelions. This type of flower spans one of the longest flowering seasons, germinating in the spring, dying off in the summer, and returning in the fall. A single flower can produce as many as 400 seeds that can be carried as far as 5 miles from where they initially originated.

Mulan and Women's Engagement in War

Welcome to SciFi or Fact, where we strive to find an intersection between science fiction and STEM. In this article, we will explore and dissect the popular animated film Mulan, set in imperial China. 

The original 1998 film depicts a young woman named Fa Mulan, who shatters social barriers by secretly going to war in her father’s place, as he is frail and Mulan is concerned that he will not make it out of the battle with the Huns. At a deeper level, the movie highlights the role of a woman who shatters societal barriers and assumes a more dominantly male role: going to war. But did women participate in wars and war-like activities in ancient China/Mongolia?

This question can be answered through an anthropological and archeological lens. Scientists now have proof that more than one female warrior (in Inner Mongolia and Upper China, where Mulan is based) did participate in war. One's skeletal structure can tell a lot about how one lives. For example, skeletons derived from persons of different ages can display degenerative bone changes and can be an indicator of diseases like gout and rheumatoid arthritis. Also, as people exercise, tiny ridges appear on bone surfaces and can serve as an indicator of how active someone was when they were alive. What’s more, trained specialists can identify a person’s gender just from looking at skeletal remains. Christine Lee, a famed bioarchaeologist (a field in anthropology), found traces of female skeletons in Northern China and Mongolia that showed indications of activities like horseback riding and archery, typical of soldiers at the time. This landmark finding indicates that women may have fought side-by-side with men. 

Scientific analysis aside, Mulan's depiction of a young woman of color breaking gender boundaries was inspirational for many young girls. However, western ideologies have seeped into the media to the point that true Asian stories are not told accurately. This has the effect of perpetuating stereotypes that can marginalize disadvantaged groups. It is up to us to stand up and share unbiased information about ourselves and the communities we belong to, especially marginalized ones. This way, we can attempt to eradicate harmful stereotypes that seek to subvert the march towards progress and racial equity.
