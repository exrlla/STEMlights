The Model Minority Myth

The model minority myth is the notion that Asian Americans are polite, hard-working, law-abiding citizens who have worked themselves up to success. It preys on the epitome of the American dream; anyone can ‘make it’ in the United States. However, though many do not see an issue with these stereotypes as they are perceived as ‘good’, it assumes that the Asian American identity is homogeneous and universal. This concept has also been historically used to perpetuate racism among other minority groups. 

Asian Americans are often lumped into one group, ignoring the diversity within what constitutes an Asian American. For example, for every dollar a cisgender white male makes in the United State, an Indian woman makes $1.21 while a Burmese woman makes $0.50 in comparison. The experiences of different groups of Asian Americans are not the same, and assuming they are can be extremely harmful.

The model minority myth has also been used to invalidate the racism Asians face in the United States. The United States has a long history of racism towards Asian Americans from the Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882 to Japanese Internment during World War II. This myth assumes that Asian Americans benefit from systemic privileges, while ignoring the systemic discrimination many have endured. 

Moreover, the model minority myth has been historically used to perpetuate racism against other minority groups. An infamous article written by a UC Berkeley professor in 1966 called “‘Success Story, Japanese-American Style’ is regarded as one of the most influential pieces written about Asian-Americans”. It established and solidified the stereotype of the ‘hard working’ Asian American family and contrasted it with African Americans who were and still are struggling with the effects of slavery and systemic discrimination. This myth directly and unjustly pits minorities against one another creating a racial wedge.

By identifying the harm that these stereotypes cause, raising awareness, and providing adequate education in schools, we can help mitigate the stigma and harm associated with the model minority myth.

 
 
 
 

Sharks Use Earth's Magnetic Fields for Directions

Previously, it's been known that sea turtles utilize Earth's magnetic fields as a map to guide them. However, recently, researchers have found that sharks similarly rely on Earth's magnetic fields to guide them on long journeys across the ocean. Bryan Keller, leader of the Save Our Seas Foundation project and also a researcher at the Florida State University Coastal and Marine Laboratory, suggests that magnetic fields are "nature's GPS." To continue this research into the effect of magnetic fields on animals, Keller also mentions that he'd be interested in exploring how changes in magnetic fields from anthropogenic sources like submarine cables could affect sharks. 

 

Health Effects of Radiation from Chernobyl

In a study by the National Cancer Institute, researchers delved into the potential health effects of exposure to ionizing radiation from the Chernobyl accident in 1986 through two studies using genomic tools. The first study found no evidence that genetic changes occurring from radiation would pass down generations. However, the second study displayed that children or fetuses that were exposed to radiation from the Chernobyl disaster and developed thyroid cancer experienced certain genetic changes in their tumors. By using specimens from the Chernobyl Tissue Bank, this research sets a precedent for future exploration in this STEM field and helps to prevent potential public health crises.

Potential Experimental Drug Against Alzheimer's Disease 

Scientists from the Albert Einstein College of Medicine have created an experimental drug that has shown to reverse major symptoms of Alzheimer's disease in mice. Although the effects of this drug on mice might not necessarily be the same for humans, this finding could have a positive influence on future Alzheimer's research. This drug works by revitalizing a certain cell-cleansing mechanism that digests and recycles unwanted proteins. By doing this, the drug reverses key symptoms of Alzheimer's in mice such as walking ability, memory, depression, and anxiety, allowing these affected mice to resemble the healthy, control mice.

Dr. Isabella Aiona Abbott

Hello STEMLights readers, happy Asian American and Pacific Island Heritage Month! Today, we will be discussing the life and achievements of Dr. Isabella Aiona Abbott. Dr. Abbott was the first native Hawaiian woman to earn a Ph.D in science, particularly in botany. Her work in ethnobotanics (the study of regional plants and their relation to a particular culture or group of people) have influenced marine botany among Pacific Islander and other Indigenous populations for decades.

Born Isabella Kauakea Yau Yung Aiona in Hana, Maui in June 1919, Dr. Abbott spent her childhood garnering interest in edible seaweed around the Hawaiian Islands. Graduating from the Kamehameha School system in 1937, she went on to earn her Bachleor’s in botany from the University of Hawaii at Manoa, with a Master’s in botany from the University of Michigan under her belt a year later. By 1950, Aiona had successfully earned her Ph.D in botany from the University of California, Berkeley, becoming the first native Hawaiian woman to earn a Ph.D in science.

After earning her Ph.D, she married her classmate Donald Abbott and started a family in California. As he taught at Stanford University, Dr. Aiona Abbott raised her children and also studied seaweed and algae around the Monterey Peninsula, until she began teaching at Stanford in 1966. By 1972, she had become the first woman in the Stanford biological sciences department to become a full-time professor.

Dr. Abbott wrote many books throughout her lifetime, including a cookbook containing recipes for cakes made with algae components that were native to the California area. She wrote seven other books and about 150 research papers throughout her lifetime as well, and discovered around 200 species of algae. She continued to lecture at Stanford, as well as her alma mater in Hawaii, consistently studying the connections between plants endemic to California and Hawaii and how they shape local culture. These achievements resulted in her receiving accolades such as Darbaker Prize and the Lifetime Achievement Award. She also has a genus of algae named in her honor, known as Abbottella. 

Dr. Abbott retired in 1982 as a Professor of Botany and passed away in 2010. Her legacy as a pioneer continues to live on.

Intestines Used for Breathing in Mammals

Rodents and pigs both share the unique capability of using their intestines during the process of respiration, the action of breathing, which is also commonly observed among aquatic organisms. After thorough research, researchers highlighted how oxygen gas (oxygenated liquid) delivered through the rectum served as a provision for the crucial rescue of two mammalian models experiencing respiratory complications.  

 

Celiac Disease Awareness Month

Celiac disease is a severe genetic autoimmune disease known to cause damage to the villi of the small intestine. This disease ultimately interferes with the body's ability to accept and process nutrients. Approximately 1 in every 133 Americans has celiac disease, representing 1% of the American population. No pharmaceutical treatments/cures are available, and the best approach to treatment is the practice of a 100% gluten-free diet.

How Do We Read Code?

Photo by JohnsonMartin on Pixabay: A section of a python code program. Programming languages have certainly improved since their inception, but the question remains: how do humans cognitively perceive code?

Computer science, the study of computers and computing, has experienced colossal changes over the last half-millennium. Computing machines require a set of instructions to run, and thus computer programming has grown in tandem with computer science. Popular programming languages like Python, C#, C++, and ScratchJr have emerged and have aided in the creation of ubiquitous applications. Still, on the cognitive level, computer code comprehension remains a mystery. What goes into computational thinking or an algorithmic approach towards problem-solving?

Some experts consider programming languages to be more math-oriented, and as such, often group them with science-oriented fields like mathematics and engineering. In this case, programming languages may evoke the Multiple Demand (MD) system (part of the frontal and parietal area of the human brain), recruited during math, logic, problem-solving, and executive tasks. Others view computer programming as another natural language, arguing that the structure of programming languages closely resembles that of natural languages such as English. In this case, processing programming languages may activate the language system, evoked when processing (reading and writing) natural languages.

Both natural and programming languages rely on smaller building blocks to represent larger quantities of information. Keywords in programming languages are written in natural languages and use concepts that are familiar to natural languages. It is not uncommon to find mathematical characters such as +, -, /, and * inside particular codes. With these arguments in mind, it remains unclear which part of the brain is recruited by computer code comprehension.

Some researchers at MIT’s Department of Brain and Cognitive Sciences sought to investigate which particular brain region is activated during code comprehension, and by extension, whether programming languages can be classified with natural languages. For this experiment, the researchers utilized two very different programming languages – Python (a more general-purpose, script-based language) and Scratch Jr (a low-level and more visual programming language often taught to children). Surprisingly, the study found that code comprehension recruited both hemispheres of the MD system and little to no areas of the language system, suggesting that neither the view of coding as a pure logic exercise nor as a natural language are completely correct. After all, math and logic recruit the left side of the MD system and, despite the user-friendly and easy-to-read nature of Python and ScratchJr and their structural similarity to natural languages, the brain did not recruit its language system.

Studying individual anatomical differences can in some way help globally shape the education system. The teaching of computer science will need to be re-conceptualized to model how best our brains respond to the acquisition of programming languages. For instance, studies could help determine the best age to start learning programming languages or developing computer science courses taught in English in a manner that benefits non-native English speakers.

The Effects of Virtual Learning

Photo by Glenn Carstens-Peters on Unsplash

As COVID-19 cases decline and restrictions ease, many schools are looking to make a full return by Fall 2021. After over a year of virtual learning, many students are left feeling burnt out, and many individuals are asking themselves: What are the effects of virtual learning?

Virtual learning is not new and has been used by higher education as a way to make school more accessible for a number of years. However, the effects of long-term virtual learning in students K-12 have not been researched previously.

The effectiveness of online learning can depend on the student. According to EducationWeek, "Students who struggle in in-person classes are likely to struggle even more online." However, if a student, with access to the necessary technological and academic resources, is proactive in learning the material, they likely can gain the same amount of information from an online class. 

Though, the difference in learning styles between online and in-person schooling could contribute to a decrease in learning for virtual students. With the advent of online school, passive learning like skimming, watching videos, and listening to lectures has become more common and has shown to drop information retention. On the other hand, in-person learning promotes active learning such as engaging in conversations, working on problem sets, and completing group projects which can contribute to more retention of the subject material. 

In general, standardized test scores this year have shown a slight decrease in math performance. Although this metric can be used to demonstrate the ineffectiveness of virtual learning, we must take this measurement with a grain of salt since this is only a small indicator of academic change. It also misleadingly assumes that every student learning digitally was testing since not every student had equal access to technology and academic resources.

Overall, we do not know the long-term effects of online learning, especially on the younger generation (ie: students in kindergarten and early elementary). This period of schooling is usually a time in which children can connect with other students their age and gain the fundamental education that will impact them for the rest of their life. Thus, not knowing the repercussions of virtual learning could have many negative consequences on future generations. Unfortunately, we won’t truly see the effects that the pandemic and virtual learning has had on education for years to come, but hopefully, our education system can continue to evolve and adapt beyond that point.

 
 
 
 

Some Mammals Can Breathe With Their Intestines

Many aquatic organisms like sea cucumbers, loaches, and catfish breathe using their intestines to survive in low-oxygen conditions. Recently, researchers have found that some rats, mice, and pigs also use this intestinal breathing technique. By designing a ventilation system that administers oxygen through the rectum, scientists discovered that with the ventilation system, 75% of mice survived 50 minutes of normally lethal low-oxygen conditions, compared to the 0% that survived 11 minutes without the ventilation system. This ventilation system, if scaled up, could be able to treat human patients experiencing respiratory failure.

 

Countering Muscle Mass Loss

Aging can cause a loss of muscle mass. To counter this, the Salk Institute found that using molecular compounds from stem-cell research could increase the regeneration of muscle cells, at least in mice. These molecular compounds, called Yamanaka factors, are transcription factors which control the copying of DNA. Adding Yamanaka factors in mice reduced levels of the Wnt4 protein and led to activated muscle stem cells, causing muscle regeneration. In the future, this monumental research could be used in developing techniques for tissue and organ regeneration.

Dark Matter Map Reveals Structures Connecting Galaxies

A dark matter map, created by an international team of scientists through machine learning, revealed many filamentary structures connecting multiple galaxies. According to Donghui Jeong, a co-author of the study, this map “opens up a new chapter of cosmological study.” Astronomers and astrophysicists can potentially study the distribution of dark matter and dive deeper into the aforementioned filamentary structures between galaxies. This model and these studies could help us see what the universe could look like billions of years into the future, and it could also do the reverse by displaying the state of the universe billions of years before the present time.

Ron Toomer

thefamouspeople.com

Hello STEMLights readers, today is the posthumous birthday of Ron Toomer, a remarkable roller coaster creator during his lifetime.

Ronald Valentine Toomer was born May 31, 1930 in Pasadena, California. Raised in the Golden State, he enlisted in the U.S Army in 1952. After returning from his deployment to Germany, he enrolled in the University of Nevada at Reno, graduating with a Bachelor’s in Science in mechanical engineering in 1961. 

After graduation, Toomer took a job at the Hercules Powder Company as a mechanical engineer, eventually transferring to Thermatest Laboratories in order to aid in the testing of heat shield sensors for NASA’s Apollo Space program in the 1960s. In 1965, he took a job at the Arrow Development Company creating the steel material necessary for the Runway Mine Ride, the world’s premiere all-steel roller coaster. In 1966, it opened at Six Flags Over Texas. In 1975, he helped design the Roaring 20’s Corkscrew at Knott’s Berry Farm in Southern California. 

Although he enjoyed crafting with wood privately, carving wooden car kits and wooden waterfowl, today, Toomer is widely known for his mechanical engineering of the steel roller coasters mentioned above. He aided or designed about 93 roller coasters throughout his extensive career, though helped design non-coaster rides as well. These included the Pirates of the Carribean ride in Disneyland and part of the Six Flags Magic Mountain layout. He even developed some of the first upside-down roller coaster elements. 

Inducted into the International Association of Amusement Parks and Attractions Hall of Fame in 2000, Toomer was a distinguished mechanical engineer. Eventually becoming President of the Arrow Development Company until his retirement, the company attempted to develop a pipeline coaster under his leadership.

Ron Toomer passed away in September of 2011. He lives on through his incredible achievements in mechanical engineering and roller coaster design.

New Space Race Raises Concerns

On April 29, 2021, the Chinese space station launched a rocket, also known as a Long March 5B core booster, into space. It stood at roughly 108 feet tall and weighed over 40,000 pounds. The rocket drifted through space aimlessly after its fuel had run out until gravity drew it back to Earth.

On May 8, the world came to know of the large remnants of a Chinese rocket that tumbled out of control and fell into the Indian Ocean near the Maldives. The rocket traveled at about 18,000 miles per hour. The re-entry of the rocket over the Arabian Peninsula was reported by the US Space Command. However, it remains unclear whether the debris hit land or the ocean. The arrival of the rocket ended days of uncertainty about where the rocket would land. Even though 70% of the Earth is made up of water, the rocket could have descended on land, which makes this all the more dangerous. It is alarming to note that the chances of the rocket landing in a densely populated area were unlikely but not impossible. 

The entire situation sparked criticism from the United States, particularly from NASA, for the lack of transparency by the Chinese space program. China has been a relative newcomer to space exploration, having launched its first spacecraft in 1970, with the Soviet Union did so in 1957 and the United States in 1958 by comparison. However, China has rapidly advanced in the space race in recent years, becoming the first country to land on the far side of the moon in 2019 and effectively collecting lunar rocks in 2020. As a result, Beijing often criticizes Western nations and the media for holding Chinese authorities to different standards. They also frequently respond with a nationalistic tone to any criticism, describing it as a malicious attempt to "smear China." Others have pointed out that this is an international issue, considering the US' previous uncontrolled landings.

There are still many unanswered questions, including how the Chinese government let this problem get out of hand. Last year, a separate Long March 5B core fell uncontrollably over the Atlantic Ocean off the West African coast, which shows that this may become a trend. 

In the years ahead, China is projected to produce even more unplanned rocket re-entries considering its launch schedule. Jonathan McDowell, an astrophysicist at the Center for Astrophysics in Cambridge, Massachusetts, who tracks the launches and re-entries of space objects, stated, “An ocean reentry was always statistically the most likely. It appears China won its gamble… But it was still reckless.”

Marsha P. Johnson

Portrait by Dan Lacey on Flickr

Greetings from STEMchats, and we want to wish our readers a happy Pride Month! In this newsletter, we will be talking about the LGBTQIA+ community, its impact on our world health, and in particular, focus on the icon named Marsha P(ay it no mind) Johnson. 

Commonly known as “BLACK Marsha,” she is most famously known for playing a significant role in the 1969 Stonewall Riots. She was a prominent figure in the riots that drew massive amounts of attention to the lack of civil rights granted to those of the LGBTQ+ community. 

Johnson was born in 1945 and saw the becoming of the LGBTQ+ and Civil Rights Movement. She founded the Street Transvestite (now Transgender) Action Revolutionaries in 1970 as well as the Gay Liberation Front. She modeled for Andy Warhol and performed as a drag queen, a role that was often unspoken of or frowned upon. She was also at the forefront of advocating for the AIDS Coalition to Unleash Power, aiming to end the AIDS epidemic.

AIDS at one point in time was considered a global epidemic. The AIDS pushback has been slowly growing since the 1980s, part of the advocacy that Johnson paved the way for. By 2006, much of the world had come to realize the importance of recognition and discussion regarding global epidemics to keep people safe. Johnson will not live to see her efforts in advocacy against AIDS, but she continues to inspire people today.

Her legacy was initially widely ignored, swept under waves of stigma against people of color and members of the LGBTQ community, but thanks to recent efforts, Marsha’s name is now renowned as the one who spread awareness and fought for the rights of those in her community — but also as one who raised her voice to protect others against an epidemic.
 

 
 
 
 

Elephants and Manipulation of Air through their Trunks

Through recent research, it has been identified that elephants go through a process of dilating their nostrils to open up room for air that can be stored in their trunks. This natural practice would ultimately allow the elephants to store up to nine liters of water in their trunks, which can suck up as much as three liters of water per second. These new findings inspired researchers to figure out ways in which robots could be used to manipulate air with the purpose of moving or holding certain things.

 

Young T. Rexes vs Adult T. Rexes: Comparing the Force of their Bites

Although scientists have gone through experiments in the past to determine the force of a T. Rex’s bite, the strength of a younger tyrannosaur’s chomp was recently discovered. Fossils with such bite marks have allowed researchers to test the force with which the marks were created. Results suggested that although the force was one-sixth of an adult T. Rex’s bite, the measured force equated to that of a living hyena.

The Bilingual Advantage

Photo by Arkyan on Wikimedia Commons: Map showing multilingual nations. Purple indicates nations that have more than one official language, green indicates nations with one official language but functionally multilingual, blue indicates nations with no official language and multiple languages in practice.

Whether it appears in pop culture through the use of Trigedasleng in "The 100" or Dothraki in "Game of Thrones," or whether it appears in real life through everyday use of English, Spanish, German, Russian, Chinese, and more, language serves one ultimate role – linking people together. There are roughly 6,500 languages are spoken in the world today, and some of them are going extinct. Still, considering this linguistic diversity, another concept of interest is how people who master two or more languages (bilingual and multilingual, respectively) are different from people who have mastered only one language (monolinguals). Indeed, the fields of cognitive psycholinguistics and linguistics have emerged and grown as a result. So, an ultimate question in today’s research arises, “Are there special (cognitive) advantages for bilingual and multilingual people?”

Simply stated, bilingualism refers to the continuous use of two languages. Bilingualism is complicated to define because of its many definitive facets such as context, proficiency, and purpose. For instance, when considering context, there is simultaneous bilingualism where an individual is bilingual by growing up simultaneously using two languages. There is also sequential bilingualism where an individual is exposed to the second language after gaining fluency in his or her first language.

In one study conducted by researchers from the Center for Brain and Cognition, Pompeu Fabra University, Barcelona, Spain, they investigated whether active bilingualism, continuous use of two languages, may protect against cognitive decline. The research sought to lay to rest the conundrum of whether the bilingual advantage might depend on the type and degree of bilingualism.

Although the researchers again proved that bilingualism was a significant factor in boosting CR and hence slowing down senile dementia in the case of MCIs, they could not replicate the conclusions set forth by previous researchers that bilingualism, too, protects against the onset of Alzheimer's Disease (AD). In their study, the researchers found out that there was a cap or point at which the underlying pathology in AD is so severe that any possible cognitive (bilingual) advantage would no longer be maintained, and individuals with both high and low levels of CR would show the same cognitive decline.

If the bilingual advantage is as definitive in preventing cognitive decline in dementia cases, such as Alzheimer’s, as in other diseases like Parkinson’s, it would mean the promotion of global linguistic diversity even in predominantly monolingual areas. Therefore, to some extent, it would help if many people throughout the world were exposed to foreign languages, and then, perhaps, people might gain deeper insight and appreciation for other cultures.
