Crisis in Afghanistan

“Refugees are people who have fled war, violence, conflict or persecution and have crossed an international border to find safety in another country,” according to the UN Refugee Agency. Refugees are often unable or reluctant to return to their country of origin because of a well-founded fear of conflict on account of race, religion, nationality, or political opinion. Internally displaced people and refugees are some of the most vulnerable people in the world. As Kofi Annan, a former UN Secretary-General puts it, “Internal displacement is the great tragedy of our time. The internally displaced people are among the most vulnerable of the human family.”

There are nearly six million Afghans who have been displaced from their homes. About three million of the six million Afghans are refugees. Afghan refugees are the third-largest displaced population in the world. For over 4 decades, Afghans have endured conflict, natural disasters, and poverty, which has led to food insecurity. Fortunately, Afghan refugees have been welcomed and supported by neighboring community members in Pakistan and Iran.

The onset of the COVID-19 pandemic has exacerbated the living conditions of some of these refugees and displaced more Afghans. Many need urgent assistance with food, water, shelter, and medicine. 

Currently, the country is facing a crisis with the Taliban. The Taliban is a radical group that rose to power in 1994 in the aftermath of the retreat of Soviet Union forces five years before. They imposed their policies on the people of Afghanistan by resorting to public punishments including floggings, amputations, and terror attacks. The leaders are seen as social pariahs who have spent several years running, hiding (from Afghan law enforcement and U.S. forces), and being arrested. 

Many people have reservations about the Taliban’s plan to be a progressive group since they regained control of the country in mid-August. Years ago, when the Taliban was in control, they prohibited women and girls from seeking employment or attending school. After the Taliban was overthrown 20 years ago, Afghan women and girls made significant strides in education. However, many of them are concerned that their achievements will be discarded and that the country will regress. The Taliban is trying to convince them that things will change, but evidence has shown that they are imposing their old regimes.

On August 26, 2021, ISIS-K or ISIL launched a terror attack on an airport in Kabul. At least 13 US service members and more than 60 Afghans are dead. At least 140 Afghans and 18 Americans have been injured. President Biden and U.S. forces have been in a race to evacuate U.S. forces and Afghans seeking asylum before the situation gets worse. Nearly 3 in 4 Americans believe that it is their duty to help Afghans who fear persecution from the Taliban.

Many Birds Begin Distinguishing Songs Prior to Hatching

Over the years, scientists have tried to closely examine how early living beings begin to perceive sound. In the instance of birds, it has become obvious that they learn to perceive songs well before hatching. Many bird species like the fairy wrens (Malurus cyaneus) learn unique tunes while remaining unhatched, which helps the mother distinguish between her nest and others. Because the embryonic heart rate in humans and birds is known to drop following a stimulus, scientists wanted to test whether this was the case in different species of unhatched birds. After testing several species of birds, it was discovered that their embryos had a slower heart rate and that these species began perceiving sound as embryos.

 

World's Tallest Tree in Danger of Burning

Following the California KNP Complex Fire initiated through lightning strikes, Sequoia National Park is being threatened as these fires approach its area. Home of some of the largest trees in the world, like the 275-foot General Sherman, the national park has begun to wrap fireproof blankets around some of the oldest sequoias marked to be around 2,700 years. As of recent reports, the fire has blazed through over 9,365 acres of land while still continuing to grow. Although wildfires can help with regeneration by enriching the soil and burning up competing plants, this type of wildfire fueled by our world’s climate change implies an issue deeper than just a natural fire.

Bleeding Tooth Fungus

The ‘bleeding tooth fungus’ is regarded as a Hydnellum fungus species and is one of the most unique-looking fungi in the world. Its most recognizable feature is the red color fluid oozing from its small pores, which is often used as a dye in textiles thanks to the atromentin chemical that gives its pigmentation. The Hydnellum fungus can be found in North American coniferous forests, in addition to some European countries (Italy, Germany, Scotland). The fruit bodies from which the fluid oozes can reach a height of 10.5 cm (4.1 in) while the stem can reach a length of 5 cm (2.0 in). Although the bleeding tooth fungus is not poisonous, it contains a light odor and has a bitter taste to it which makes it inedible.

Katerina Manoff

We have come a long way in terms of freedom, yet not everyone holds an equal share in it.  Heroes of all ages have fought to mend the gaps in opportunity, socio-economic status, and technology, armed with nothing more than endless patience and compassion. One of these heroes is Katerina Manoff, a 34 year old Ukrainian woman who created a program that “pairs Ukrainian students with English speakers for free online conversation practice and cross-cultural connection.”

This program, ENGin, is meant to promote better English fluency. It would give English-fluent students opportunities to earn volunteer service hours and to learn about Ukrainian culture. She hopes that with better English fluency, Ukrainian youth will be culturally competent and have better access to professional opportunities. “So, ultimately, we are using English as a tool to transform a nation, spurring economic growth and helping build connections to the global community.”

ENGin has been Manoff’s passion project for years in the making. She tells STEMlights that she loves running ENGin because of the constant variety in tasks she faces every day. “No day is the same and I'm learning a ton along the way.” She’s worked in the educational field for a decade, bouncing around with all sorts of organizations. Her passion for writing and business has even led to several publications and a career as an investment banker. Ultimately, Manoff never stopped searching for the perfect career. She tells us that as a woman, she understands the struggles between choosing family over career. Figuring out the balance between growing professionally and pursuing personal dreams can be extremely difficult when one has kids to take care of.

Her advice to our readers is to take “to invest the time in deeply understanding yourself - your dreams, your talents, your temperament - and use that information to forge your own career path. ” There are no right or wrong career choices, regardless of the opinions of others, only careers that fit or don’t fit. She hopes that people pursue the things that they love doing and to live authentically by their own desires.

National Security Association Internships

The National Security Association of the United States’ Department of Defense has opened multiple student programs for around thirty different fields, as well as a multitude of scholarships. There are options for high school, college, and even a few graduate student options available. From Health Promotion & Wellness to Information Assurance to even Industrial and Organizational Psychology, the wide variety of programs the NSA offers may align with your own interests. Some of them take place in summer while others are open to be started in the spring or school terms as work studies. Although the application deadlines range, some of them close soon this fall.
